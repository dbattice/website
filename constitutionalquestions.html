Apportioning the House

Fixing the House: proportional representation, apportionment, expand the House, end primaries, and fix redistricting

**For the record basically any system is better than FPTP without primaries. It would be better to be like Canada, and even better to be Australia with AV. Or just straight up two rounds like in France. But if you switch to proportional then there is a decent case to use MMP like Germany, or STV like Ireland. But I prefer open lists with members chosen based purely on votes on each list because it is simple and give parties the least amount of power while retaining open lists. Jack Santucci layed out clearly why open lists seem to be prefered, and some of this I got from him.**

Assuming we adopt proportional representation, how should that be done? How many constituencies and how people are elected from each? What, if any, are the leveling mechanisms? What is the electoral threshold for getting seats? STV, MMP, open lists, or closed lists? Usually there is an electoral threshold of 2% to 5%. I think the House should start with a threshold of 4% or 5% to prevent to much fragmentation. But then how do you allocate votes with each district or nationally? I think we should use open lists in the United States. This means that voters choose their favorite candidate from their favorite party, then seats are allocated first to each party, then to the candidates who received the most votes within each party (panachage). More about this later, but I think there should also be some kind of national leveling seats, especially because of how few representatives there can be in small states. Open lists are my favorite because everyone thinks in party membership anyway, and it allows very easy access to minority views via a new party and the threshold to entering the legislature is low that as long as about 3-5% back you, you get to have some input, and will likely force more negotiation because it is almost impossible for one party to win an outright majority. Generally, there is d'Hondt or Sainte-Laguë, which are both largest remainder methods, to allocate seats to each party. D'Hondt method minimizes 'wasted' votes, and slightly favors larger parties or at least parties not splitting, while Sainte-Laguë is more proportional and favors smaller parties slightly. (Single transferable vote (STV) generally uses the Droop quota or using votes/(seats + 1). Hare quota is the more straight forward version of votes/seats. I would use the Droop quota if STV is used.)

Leveling seats are fun. Spain, Italy, Sweden, Norway, and Finland all use different methods. Germany and UK examples of MMP are also different. First I want to distinguish between regional lists and with leveling seats and without. Spain is a great example of what happens when you set a minimum of seats per district (each province is a district), with no national leveling seats. The quota used for determining seats isn't relevant here because of the strength of regionalist parties in Spain. The regionalist parties in Spain generally distort the percentage of vote compared to the percentage of seats. I think this is somewhat bad because the regionalist parties can get many seats, while broadly national parties with more votes get fewer seats, specifically the Canary Coalition, Vox, Ciudadanos, and other regional parties. did a in a the two elections in 2019. Part of the disproportionality lies with the a minimum number of MPs for each district and wildly different populations in each district. Switzerland also has this system, but each canton is a district and they have a long history of the 'Magic Formula', so the disproportionality matters less anyway. For contrast, Finland basically uses the same list system with regional districts but with much more equal districts, although they still don't have a perfect correlation between seats and votes, but there is a lower variance in the disparities than in Spain. Norway is a very interesting case of lists and leveling seats. Norway gives out 150 seats based on population and area, which does have a rural bias. Each district uses open lists, and then each district has an extra seat for purposes of leveling. This leveling does take national vote counts into equalization, but the members elected in these seats still come the county list in which they were a candidate. (My opinion is that should more directly allocate seats based purely on population or have even more leveling seats to get increased proportionality.) The Norway system strikes me as a very good option that the US could emulate, especially if the senate becomes more like the Canadian Senate (basically has no influence on legislation or any other powers).

Now Sweden and Denmark. Both have districts, but also have leveling seats, so the allocation of seats is among the lowest of any system. Sweden has a threshold nationally to get leveling seats, or a threshhold a party has to reach in any one district. Denmark has 'nominating districts' and a 2% threshhold to get levling seats. If a party wins in a nominating district, they get the seat, but almost every party that meets the 2% also wins at least one nominating district. The leveling seats are then apportioned nationally. In Sweden they go through a few steps to get to the leveling seats, but basically it allocating seats in each district and then they use the national totals to allocate the additional leveling seats. (The Netherlands basically has a national list where you only need 1/150 or .667% of the national vote to get a seat. They use d'Hondt so it is not the most proportional but they already have so many parties.) Although, according to this, Denmark has single vote MMP with leveling seats, but it kind of functions like open lists.

MMP mainly used in Germany and New Zealand and subnationally in the UK. Note that many large countries use MMP or its cousins parallel voting or MMM or AMS, like Japan, Germany, Italy, Scotland and Wales, South Korea, and Mexico.) The simple version is half the seats are FPTP, and the rest are allocated proportionally to the party vote. (Oh yeah, every voter gets a vote for the FPTP seat and a vote for their favorite party.) The FPTP vote doesn't have to be for the same party and the second vote. The party seats are basically list seats, except most people are on both, and the party leaders get the top places on the list. The trickiness comes when you get to all the party seats and you still have disproportionality. I don’t like MMP because of what happens in Germany where one party wins way more local seats than they get via proportional list seats. CDU/CSU wins almost all local seats such that they have to add MPs to maintain proportionality (more on that below). So either you aren’t proportional enough or you have to add MPs every once in a while. New Zealand doesn’t have this problem but they need to add more list seats in the future. It is much simpler than STV and is still much better than the current system in the US. You could get overhang seats, where a party won more FPTP seats than their party votes say they should have, for example the CDU in 2017. Germany also had case of negative vote weight where votes for the party list could help rather than hurt that party. In both these cases Germany used to just say too bad. Then the Federal Constitutional Court said, no, you have to keep adding seats to be proportional (now overhang seats and leveling seats). This started expanding the number of members in parliament, adding over 100 members a couple of times in a row. I think mostly for practical reasons, they changed this after the 2021 election. Germany basically switched to getting rid of local FPTP winners if the party doesn't reach the threshhold or there are overhang seats, in addition to expanding the number of seats to 630 (was 598 but was frequently more than 630 after the Federal Constitutional Court ruling and the law enacted because of it. Germany's high threshhold and the new system could lead to some chaos in Bavaria in the next federal election in Germany, which will be very interesting to watch. The UK subnational parliaments start the divisors with the number of FPTP seats they won and then add seats to each party until all the designated party seats are allocated, regardless of proportionality (no overhang seats). (Some countries use or have used MMP but they pick a member of the party they want to vote for, eliminating ticketsplitting.) When they have overhang seats, they just end up not getting any list seats and divide the list seats proportionally until they have no more list seats, even if it is unproportional. New Zealand takes a different approach where the parties keep the overhang seats, but they have a fixed number of list seats so it ends up being more proportional (overhang seats only.) Following the examples here MMP with overhang and leveling is better than no leveling, which is better than AMS, which is better than FPTP, (although, again, anything is better than FPTP).

Italy's current electoral system is not great for it being a list system. The first election with the new system resulted in an outright majority for a party that only got low 40s percent of the vote. They used to use 'scorporo', which is a form of MMP, but the rules for the proportional list seats were badly written and ended up being gamed by the political parties.

There is also a positive vote transfer system where the votes for losers and surplus votes from winners then go toward allocating seats in the party votes/seats (which was used in Romania for an election or two, and for local elections in Hungary).

More fun with a proposed system in the UK called alternative vote plus. The idea is to use the alternative vote, like the House of Representatives in Australia, and then have a second 'party vote' like in Germany.

Open list voting leads to higher public goods with increasing district magnitudes, but lower with closed lists. Lower electoral volatility leads to lower public goods in both. Closed lists have less experienced candidates, who are less active/less good at campaigning and more likely to have done election fraud previously. Here is a paper by Buben and Kouba analyzing list voting in Latin America along dimensions of extremist parties, stability, party system consolidation, and corruption. They recommend moving away from personalization, by not making open lists more open voting and not moving to smaller district magnitudes (fewer members elected per district). Open lists seem to be bad for (salient) single issue parties and candidates, probably. This seems like a feature, not a bug to me though. Open list voting seems to have more localism, more pork spending and weaker party discipline. However, this paper suggests there isn’t much intraparty competition in open lists. Chang and Golden (2007) find lower corruption in open list voting systems (for districts less than 15 seats or so) than closed list elections. One last thing on closed lists is that you cannot run as an independent, but this doesn't seem like that big of a deal because you can start your own party and most people think in parties anyway.

Replacing members who die resign or otherwise leave office. Germany won't let overhang seats be filled if member doesn't serve the full term. Otherwise just go to the party list. Netherlands uses lists, but if a member in office leaves the party, the member keeps the parliamentary seat.

Fix the Senate to be truly representative of the states and to express states' power against the rest of the federal government

Looking at other countries, I am surprised we don't have an instution where states can exert power within the federal government. Canada and Australia have councils of premiers where the premiers of each state/province and the prime minister/cabinet meet and work out agreements. Germany's Bundesrat or 'upper chamber' is composed of members chosen entirely by state cabinets and each state's members must exercise their power together, but also get no say over non-state affairs. Czech Senate must consent to treaties, electoral law and constitutional changes. Czech Senate also gets to confirm judges, not confidence in cabinet or ministers or budget and taxes. Elected by two rounds with one third elected every two years. Italy has perfect bicameralism including on confidence motions. It is dumb, do not copy. In the Netherlands, the upper chamber is chosen based on the provincial elections, but neither the chamber nor provinces are particularly important. Belgium also has its upper chamber based on regional elections (that occur at the same time as the national elections) and then those fifty senators choose another 10 senators. In the US, the closest we have to any of this is governors asking Congress or the executive branch nicely. Yes, the National Governor's association exists, but I can't tell if they have ever meaningfully done anything. In Australia, their council of state governments played a role in Covid policy. In Canada, they have worked out numerous agreements over time and have even proposed amendments. In Germany they are constantly working to hammer out deals, including a major reform in the early 2000s, but also talking through tax changes occassionally (probably due to Germany's tax sharing system, but still, that is a major deal that states directly influence.) Which is to say I think this should be a major point of potential reform. So I think we need an 'American Bundesrat' modeled after Germany or Belgium. Even an informal organization, closer to Australia, would be great, because it would be very hard for the the rest of the American government, to ignore such an expression of states unified together saying the same thing. It seems like this could be a major way out of the muddled middle of nothing happening and everyone frustrated. The other reason I like this idea is because you can immediately tell if someone is serious about a policy proposal because you can ask if they introduced it to the American Bundesrat. It is all fine and dandy to say you wish the federal government would do X, but have you made a proposal to the American Bundesrat?

Proposal to make the Senate a Bundesrat: chosen by state lower house majority. Minimum of two senators per state. Changes due to population take effect when the House sits after redistricting. Additional senators at for 1,2, 5 and every 10 million. Or just use the logarithmic formula. All senators from the same state must vote the same way. Treaties still require senate approval as do constitutional amendments (confirmed by voting public). Also federal impeachments. Czechia requires that the senate approve electoral laws too. Every vote is majority. Only absolute majority for constitutional and presidential votes (for choosing president instead of electing president). We could do more of an Australian style upper house. Each state gets the same number of seats, is directly elected, territories get some representation, and uses some proportional representation method. I still prefer to keep the decreasingly proprotional system, like in Germany. Can also do reports, investigations, and introduce legislation. I just don't think there is much of an argument to keep the Senate wildly disproportional while its members are directly elected and the Senate has so much power. If we cannot get structural reform, then the Senate should be stripped of its power via amendment. To weaken the Senate, give it the power to suspend legislation including budget and tax bills, requiring an absolute majority of members in the House to vote yes. Take away its role in confirming nominations. Take away its powers for nominations and appointments. You could even go as far as the Czech Republic and not allow the senate to vote on budgets, and its amendments or rejections of the lower house can be overtuned with an absolute majority, except for cases of treaties, constitutional amendments, and electoral laws, which sounds pretty good to me. We could also do something like requiring the majority to represent at least half of the population (via the last census), kind of like referendums in Switzerland with a double majority requirement.

Make the president weak again

**I started writing this well before Trump v. United State came down, but really it seems to me that it mainly gives urgency to this problem rather than the hypothetical future problem that is easy to avoid with one amendment.**

Curb presidential power/make the president a figurehead. Presidential power is written weak, but has gotten stronger. other presidents can propose or amend laws, call elections, are given immunity, and can challenge the constitutionality of a law. The US president is weak on paper, but has definitely acquired more power and importance over time, de facto.

This gets some things wrong, like the House confirming judicial nominees (the Senate does that), but the overall point of 'we don't need a president' is absolutely true.

John Tyler took the presidency when William Henry Harrison died. He took the oath, used the powers, and moved to the White House. His political opponenets never really accepted this, and it really wasn't confirmed until the 25th Amendment. I thought I heard or read that he didn't respond to letters unless he was called president? And signed everything as president.

There is precendent for changing presidential ticket after a convention, as in 1972 when Thomas Eagleton was replaced. (He then got re-elected to the Senate two years later!)

Nelson Polsby has a very good book on how how primaries have weakend grass roots politics, made the presidency more candidate focused, and likely decreased the average quality of the candidates nominated. Also gave more power to non-official party organization (interest groups/activists?).

These are relatively centrist national security people, saying we have no idea what the president is not immune from, and it is crazy that so much would be immune. Biden, to his credit will not take advantage of the now extensive immunity or apparent immunity given by SCOTUS. Although, I wonder if he should do the most minor thing that is clearly crazy a president can get away, just to push the point and urge Congress to do something. Or the people to do an amendment again.

There was a weaker president, such as in the 1990s most Senate democrats wanted to control use of force for Gulf War, not necessarily caring about if US went to war, but later they aquiesced to presidential dominance on military force questions, from Helen Thompson and Tom McTague on These Times. US president is too strong, part I, part II, and part III. David Sligar once commented on twitter: why even have a head of state? Just give the prime minister or speaker of the legislature any roles necessary and make them accountable to parliament. My answer is to have the head of state chosen by parliament whether it be a governor-general or president. Or go full Switzerland and have a collective executive. Interfluidity made a good distinction between a representative of all versus a representation of all. But why do we need one for all representation anyway? Just be Switzerland with a collective head of state or choose a governor-general like former British colonies or some systems where the parliament chooses the president.

France has a semi-presidential system. This is a weaker president, but not just ceremonial. The president appoints the prime minister (but the prime minister can be dismissed by the National Assembly), calls elections, does many things related to foreign policy, including the military, has emergency powers, can grant pardons, and has immunity while in office.

President of Austria appoints and dismisses the cabinet, same for state governments and other officials, appoints judges, signs laws (but can refer them to the court for constitutionality), signs treaties, and has some emergency powers. The president can also call elections unilaterally.

The President of Bulgaria has a few weird powers, like being able to give or revoke citizenship and refugee status and rename cities, but has the normal powers of appointing prime minister and other officials, can pardon people, temporarily veto laws, emergency powers, calls elections, and gives awards.

The President of the Czech Republic can veto laws but can be overridden by absolute majority of members, calls elections, immunity, and ceremonial powers. They require a minimum number of signatures to run or the support of a minimum number of senators or deputies.

The Icelandic president is weak. The president can do normal appointing the cabinet and other officials and dissolving of parliament, can submit bills to parliament, can reject a bill which is then submitted to a referendum by the public, some emergency powers, and can grant pardons and amnesty. If the president cannot do his duty or leaves office early, the head of the legislature, Supreme Court, and the prime minister collectively share the role.

Finland has a weak president too. The president makes appointments, is the commander in chief has emergency powers, and can do one-off pardons. Of course, he or she also appoints the prime minister and cabinet. The president can veto or wait out a bill, but generally doesn't.The president algo grants awards, honors, and medals.

President of Greece is basically ceremonial. Ireland also has a ceremonial weak president, but has a lot of symbolic roles.

President of Estonia is quite weak and only seems to do the things a constiutial monarch would. The president of Estonia can make proposals for amendments, laws, and emergency powers/martial law to the Riigikogu (Estonian Parliament).

President of Latvia is similar to Estonia but has slightly more power. The president of Estonia is explicilty not the chief executive, even in name only. The President of Latvia loses his job if he calls for an election and the people reject it in a referendum.

(Taiwan uses FPTP to elect its president.) Austria, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Finland, France, and Iceland use the two round system to elect their presidents. Ireland uses ranked choice voting to elect its president. Estonia, Greece, and Latvia have the parliament choose the president, and the president is very weak. Georgia, Germany, and Italy elect the president via special convention made of members of parliament and other government officials or people chosen by lower levels of government.

If the president leaves office before the end of the term, the and the president of the senate takes over in France, and Germany. In Austria, a trio from the lower house takes over until a new president it elected.

The Swiss collective executive is a council of seven people chosen by all members of the parliament and they are voted on individually. The parties have agreed to the ‘magic formula’, where the three "biggest" parties each get two people in the council, and the fourth "biggest" party gets the seventh seat. The chair of the council, or president, rotates every year. The president does not have special powers or authority. There is also a vice president who generally serves as the president for the following year.

President of Germany can do the same appointments, calling of elections, veto bills (though very rarely and only because the president believed it was unconstitutional or required the Bundesrat to approve it), signs all bills, awards, individual pardons, and some emergency powers.

In Italy, the president can appoint senators for life, approve laws or send it to parliament for reconsideration, call referendums, appointing the prime minister, cabinet, and other officials, emergency powers, can dissolve regional governments and legislatures, immunity for most crimes, granting pardons and commutations, and granting honors and awards.

Side note, the Governors-General of former UK colonies all basically do nothing. I want to highlight Canada and Australia because they are federal countries like the United States. The Governor-General in Canada just formally appoints people, gives royal assent to bills, calls elections, and a few other ceremonial things. They are chosen by the prime minister, although recently a committee or advisory group was used to inform the decision more recently. The Governor-General in Australia has basically the same power as in Canada but seemingly has more discretion? They also are nominated (appointed) by the prime minister.

In a unicameral system I think proposing laws works. In a bicameral system with different constituencies or electoral systems in each house, it seems better not allow the president to propose laws. Calling elections unilaterally seems bad except after a period of impasse. Otherwise just have it done after a given amount of time or with the prime minister asking for them. Veto should be removed, especially in bicameral systems. Immunity shouldn’t exist, but if it does, statute of limitations is suspended while in office and they are definitely not immune out of office. I think it is probably a good idea to let the president refer laws to courts or in some way challenge constitutionality, again, less necessary for bicameral legislatures. Vetoes are tricky, but probably shouldn’t be allowed. The Bulgarian and Italian way of a veto sending the bill back to parliament for reconsideration I think is good. Decrees and executive orders should be done by the prime minister or congress, not the president. There may be something where it must be the president but should be as minimal as possible. Declaring a state of emergency is probably okay if the cabinet (parliamentary cabinet) must agree, but should not give the president any extra power without congressional or parliamentary power that can be revoked at any time. As far as term limits go, usually a president has up to two terms, but sometimes one like in Mexico. There are a few weak presidents that can serve many terms. Some countries don't allow consecutive terms. Overall, I think the stronger the president is, the less terms they should have. (By the way, there is a paper by Cheibub saying that presdientialism works well when the president always fears he or she might not be reelected. We obvious are not following the implications of that.) Term length is usually four, five or six years. Again, the weaker the president, the less this matters. I prefer four or five years, especially if the legislature is elected every four or five years and is mostly coincident with presidential terms. Maybe the president should not be able to hold any other office afterward leaving the presidency? Not even state or local offices. Just give them a very cushy pension.

So given all of these examples, I think there are a few viable and preferred changes the US could or should make. The first thing would probably be to change the presdiential veto to a suspensory veto, or have to provide a reason why it is unconstitutional. Basically congress has final say on legislation with a pure absolute majority of members voting yes on a law. Get rid of presidential primariesThe next thing is to change the election of the president. It would be best to not directly elect the president. I think something like Germany's convention of states choosing members, and the members of both houses, and then they just vote a few times until they get a majority. This seems really good and seems likely to elect moderate, competent, and uncorrupt elder statesmen. If there must be direct election, obvious ranked choice would be the best but seems highly impractical, so two-round system it is. The next thing is to make it easier to remove a corrupt, treasonous, or otherwise uncapable president. I think a three fifths of both houses of congress together should be sufficient to remove a president. Then the presidential powers that are necessary to be done can be fulfilled by the majority and minority leader of the Senate and someone the speaker names who has served at least six years in the senate, or less with an absolute majority of senators agreeing. Lastly remove presidential power such that the prime minister is the executive and president is not the executive or commander in chief like Estonia. The president should also be able to do one-off pardons, amnesty, and commutations, but not mass pardons or amnesty or commutations. Alexander Hamilton thought a pardon could be used as a military thing to stop rebellions. George Mason thought it made the president too powerful. It has gotten especially powerful and less common since Gerald Ford pardoned Richard Nixon. I think the pardon is fine, but there should probably limits to pardoning ones self and people closest to the president and vice president and the cabinet. No mass pardons, no amnesty. Spain seems to have a good model of how to do pardons, based on Wikipedia. The president can only do nominations and and appointments with consent of prime minister or cabinet.

If and only if the president is directly elected in two round or with alternative vote should the president be able to propose laws to parliament. Also, if and only if directly elected the president should have to get support of at least ten percent of current legislators, rounded up (501 legislaters means 51 must publically back you).

One last oddball idea is to have a joint legislature use STV to elect all members of a collective executive like in Swizterland where the memebers collectively decide on all actions. of the collective executive.

Bad assumptions

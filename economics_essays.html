Unemployment

Unemployment is underrated by politics. There are many angles to this, but let's start with János Kornai. He was a Hungarian economist who lived in communist Hungary. He came to view central planning as a bad economic system and mathematically showed why. Basically, he showed that central planning leads to persisent shortages because of distortions of price signals. Kornai did think that non-socialist economies did have a flaw though, which is that they don't do enough to keep unemployment low. Specifically, he saw unemployment as surplus (or misallocated) labor, and thought more effort should be given to make sure that society used all of its resources efficiently, including all workers. Suppliers create surplus capacity and inventory to be able to dynamically respond to demand, even though there might be irrationally high amounts of surplus products and capacity. In his view, advertising is really a way to keep the inventory sales flowing. Governments can impede surplus, for example, in housing. Kornai saw unemployment as surplus labor, and seemed to agree with Marx’s analysis of unemployment as the ‘reserve army’. Kornai’s work definitely points to better policies to maintain full employment and UI in lieu of it to maintain aggregate demand. Sectoral unions and strong state spending are also probably ideal to help with this problem (Sidenote: he also wanted capacity planning, but not socialist planning. This was to keep production high and to help relieve bottlenecks to production ahead of time.) Another reason unemployment is underrated has to do with class. I think it is believable that the richest people have the most influence on public policy, but even if not, it should be important to know where the policy preferences are for the richest people versus everyone else, so we can see how the richest might distort policy and see if the claim that they have the most influence is true. This survey is very interesting, but one thing that stuck out to me is that, that normal people think that government should do a lot to reduce unemployment, like about two thirds. But among the millionaires, only about one third said it was an important problem. Phillips curve or Phillips line?

Is the Phillips curve a line or a curve? This seems kind of unimportant, but given the scale and consequences of excess unemployment the answer to this is incredibly important. Joe Gagnon and others at PIIE have done some work on this, and they think it is a curve, and other economists are erring when they think it is a line. Joe Gagnon talked about his paper on Macro Musings. Joe Gagnon thinks that the Phillips curve is nonlinear. I thought it was something like y=1/n^x where x>1, but apparently some people think that is it y=-m*x+b? (y is the inflation rate, and x is the unemployment rate gap.) (See this blog post by Worthwhile Canadian, which clearly says it is a curve, not a line.) Joe Gagnon cites a paper from Akerlof, Dickens, and Perry that a lower a inflation target leads to higher U* or unemployment rate and which inflation is steady, and also Joe Gagnon thinks that is where we are now. He also thinks that deviations from steady NGDP growth will show up sooner in a world with higher inflation, and that is why he likes NGDP targeting too. This is because the economy would be operating closer to the Y axis where the 1/x^n curve has a steeper slope when you change the X variable slightly. He also says something very interesting that he thinks NGP targeting is only possible with higher nominal growth targetso that way you see deviations faster. (Side note here: the Phillips curve existed until the 1970s or so. Then it became a negatively sloping line, then in the 1990s it flipped back to a curve, I guess?) Claudia Sahm doesn't think the Phillips curve is useful, because the tradeoffs of inflation and unemployment would almost certainly change over time. Also, demography, technology, and productivity will change the tradeoffs as well. Skanda Amarnath also pointed this out in the context of the 2023 disinflation, highlighting how supply chains and productivity have contributed to the disinflation, meaning the Phillips curve didn't apply in the post Covid inflation episode. I mostly agree that the trade offs are not as clear cut, although I think Gagnon is correct in the extreme case of a sudden jump in inflation. Alan Blinder on Planet Money said that he doesn't think the Phillips curve is useful since for the 1990s until 2021 unemployment swung wildly wihtout much change in inflation, so it is a broken tool. So, as an outsider I find this troubling. This concept is central so many central bankers' thinking that one has to think about it when thinking about monetary policy or even just macroeconomic policy. But they can't even figure out the relationship graphically between inflation and unemployment? Especially when the consequences are so important (see the high cost of unemployment in so many ways.) Ultimately, I support switching to NGDP targeting, which Joe Gagnon mostly agrees with, in addition to so many other people. So I think it is pretty compelling that the Phillips curve is a curve, but also is not that useful. We should focus more on NGDP and focusing on non-monetary policy to lower inflation and increase employment, or at least coordinate monetary and non-monetary actions to reduce inflation. And that is such a huge topic it deserves it own essay.

Progressive should embrace markets in some areas

This is for some areas. Some areas it doesn't matter, some areas should be socialized or heavily regulated. However, the main areas progressive should embrace markets are housing, occupational licensing, health insurance, telecoms like EU, carbon pricing instead of income taxes, energy (depends on specifics), DBCFT and VAT instead of corporate tax, end subsidization of private retirement funding (401ks etc) and expand social security, tax cars more, do more ownership stakes in business when giving them startup funds, Geogrism more and income taxes less, Green TVA? The Jones Act doesn't work anymore and doesn't achieve its goal of helping the US shipbuilding industry. Here is another article that says that repealing the Jones Act wouldn't completely devestate the industry because right now a lot of the work is done by foreign companies, and the US still makes good river barges. And that leads me to the point that it is kind of arbitrary what must be done in the US to qualify as domestic for purposes of the Jones Act. Ships can be designed abroad, several components can come from abroad, and the ship can still qualify as domestic. Meanwhile the whole point was to subsidize the shipbuilding industry so we could ramp up production with the latest and greatest technology, but who is to say that is the best way to maintain shipbuilding capacity with the best available technology? It certainly seems to me that in 2023 it is not. Maybe it would be better to fund vocational schools that teach people how to make ships well. Maybe it would be better to fund research directly on shipping technology. Maybe it would be better to regulate the ships used for certain emissions and safety standards, like we do for motor vehicles. Maybe we give tax credits if a percentage of a shipping company's cargo is carried on ships constructed in the United States, so that shipbuilders have a reason to become cost competitive with the rest of the world, but shipping companies can choose non-American ships in a way that is not as costly to consumers. We could also exempt remote locations, like the various island territories, Hawaii, Alaska, etc, from the Jones Act, or exempt certain goods, like oil, semiconductors and electronics, or food. I am just throwing ideas out there, but these seem better on the surface and deserve more exploration and research. the shipping industry. Also the dredging law and that Odd Lots episode. Spend all college funds on subsidizing students. Spend all housing money on vouchers and subsidizing construction like Vienna. Like this. A libertarian has written a good primer on why deregulation can be good, although I would take issue with alcohol (do we really want cheaper alcohol? Just socialize the companies somewhat to prevent bad incentives) and banking (we always bail out banks so maybe we should partially nationalize some banks too?) for example. On other hand, car dealerships definitely deserve more scrutiny and it would be good politcally because car dealership owners are major Republican backers and they have the money, small business status, and distributed geographic power that give it outsized influence. Kimberly Clausing also wrote a book about trade and why progressives should back it. One of the points she made is that tariffs are bad because they tend to be applied to goods that are disproportionately consumed by lower income people, and of course it is just a regressive tax, and benefits companies not consumers.

Unemployment

Unemployment is underrated by politics. There are many angles to this, but let's start with János Kornai. He was a Hungarian economist who lived in communist Hungary. He came to view central planning as a bad economic system and mathematically showed why. Basically, he showed that central planning leads to persisent shortages because of distortions of price signals. Kornai did think that non-socialist economies did have a flaw though, which is that they don't do enough to keep unemployment low. Specifically, he saw unemployment as surplus (or misallocated) labor, and thought more effort should be given to make sure that society used all of its resources efficiently, including all workers. Suppliers create surplus capacity and inventory to be able to dynamically respond to demand, even though there might be irrationally high amounts of surplus products and capacity. In his view, advertising is really a way to keep the inventory sales flowing. Governments can impede surplus, for example, in housing. Kornai saw unemployment as surplus labor, and seemed to agree with Marx’s analysis of unemployment as the ‘reserve army’. Kornai’s work definitely points to better policies to maintain full employment and UI in lieu of it to maintain aggregate demand. Sectoral unions and strong state spending are also probably ideal to help with this problem (Sidenote: he also wanted capacity planning, but not socialist planning. This was to keep production high and to help relieve bottlenecks to production ahead of time.) Another reason unemployment is underrated has to do with class. I think it is believable that the richest people have the most influence on public policy, but even if not, it should be important to know where the policy preferences are for the richest people versus everyone else, so we can see how the richest might distort policy and see if the claim that they have the most influence is true. So Page, Bartels, and Seawright got several millionaires and several ordinary people to answer a survey about whether the government should care more or less about different policy areas. This survey is very interesting, but one thing that stuck out to me is that, that normal people think that government should do a lot to reduce unemployment, like about two thirds. But among the millionaires, only about one third said it was an important problem. And in the United States, the unemployment system is not generous, not centralized, not easy to access benefits, and makes recessions worse because it leads to declines in state and local tax revenues, which means the states and local governments start laying people off, inceasing unemployment even more. Phillips curve or Phillips line?

Is the Phillips curve a line or a curve? This seems kind of unimportant, but given the scale and consequences of excess unemployment the answer to this is incredibly important. Joe Gagnon and others at PIIE have done some work on this, and they think it is a curve, and other economists are erring when they think it is a line. Joe Gagnon talked about his paper on Macro Musings. Joe Gagnon thinks that the Phillips curve is nonlinear. I thought it was something like y=1/n^x where x>1, but apparently some people think that is it y=-m*x+b? (y is the inflation rate, and x is the unemployment rate gap.) (See this blog post by Worthwhile Canadian, which clearly says it is a curve, not a line.) Joe Gagnon cites a paper from Akerlof, Dickens, and Perry that a lower a inflation target leads to higher U* or the unemployment rate at which inflation is steady, and also Joe Gagnon thinks that is where we are now. He also thinks that deviations from steady NGDP growth will show up sooner in a world with higher inflation, and that is why he likes NGDP targeting too. Joe Gagnon on Macro Musings in September 2023 missed an important point. They did not discuss labor share conflicts as part of inflation and seemed glad that the quits rate was falling, reflecting a looser labor market (aka slightly worse for workers). I wanted to yell at them that the Fed cannot control everything (nor probably should) and there is a role for fiscal policy in an ideal world. Example, housing, taxes, etc. Gagnon did make an interesting distinction between expectations and confidence around inflation and macro policy, which I think is the most defensible version of ‘inflation expectations’. They also think that R* will remain lower, even if it is temporarily higher. Gagnon thinks that with a low R*, and also to avoid the zero lower bound (and nominal rigidities), we should have an inflation target of 3%. They also reference Ricardo Reis who thinks that raising interest rates will reduce employment or wage growth as a *side effect*, but not a cause and it is not always guaranteed to happen. This is because the economy would be operating closer to the Y axis (lower unemployment rate) where the 1/x^n curve has a steeper slope when you change the X variable slightly. He also says something very interesting that he thinks NGP targeting is only possible with higher nominal growth target so that way you see deviations faster. (Side note here: the Phillips curve existed until the 1970s or so. Then it became a negatively sloping line, then in the 1990s it flipped back to a curve, I guess?) Claudia Sahm doesn't think the Phillips curve is useful, because the tradeoffs of inflation and unemployment would almost certainly change over time. Also, demography, technology, and productivity will change the tradeoffs as well. Skanda Amarnath also pointed this out in the context of the 2023 disinflation, highlighting how supply chains and productivity have contributed to the disinflation, meaning the Phillips curve didn't apply in the post Covid inflation episode. I mostly agree that the trade offs are not as clear cut, although I think Gagnon is correct in the extreme case of a sudden jump in inflation. Alan Blinder on Planet Money said that he doesn't think the Phillips curve has been useful since 1990s until 2021 because unemployment swung wildly without much change in inflation, so it is a broken tool. Chris Hughes made the case that Arthur Burns was ahead of the curve by trying non monetary measures to reduce inflation, but this was forgotten in general. Chris Hughes on Macro Musings said that Arthur Burns was focused on unions, regulations, supply side factors, etc. as the cause of inflation problems. Arthur Burns also didn’t believe in the Phillips Curve, and he thought that inflation was bad because it distorted investment decisions. Chris Hughes also says we need to build capacity to quickly roll out infrastructure and other policy measures to combat inflation, since we mostly don’t have it now. So, as an outsider I find this troubling. This concept is central so many central bankers' thinking that one has to think about it when thinking about monetary policy or even just macroeconomic policy. But they can't even figure out the relationship graphically between inflation and unemployment? Especially when the consequences are so important (see the high cost of unemployment in so many ways.) Ultimately, I support switching to NGDP targeting, which Joe Gagnon mostly agrees with, in addition to so many other people. I think it is pretty compelling that the Phillips curve is a curve, but also is not that useful. We should focus more on NGDP and on non-monetary policy levers to lower inflation and increase employment, or at least coordinate monetary and non-monetary actions to reduce inflation with minimal employment effects. And that is such a huge topic it deserves it own essay.

There are a lot of political reasons to favor NGDP targeting. First of all, it is an explicit, simple to understand mandate. Simplicity is very good in general, and it is better to have one mandate than two (or three?) that you have to balance. Psychologically NGDP targeting is better because feelings and expectations are more tied to economic behavior in a crisis than actually calculating inflation adjusted prices and realizing that even though my wages have barely gone up, I actually have increasing compensation and purchasing power far greater than I used to. (If that sounds complicated and ridiculous, that’s the point.) NGDP targeting also reduces politicization of a critical aspect of the modern economy, because the main question of the Federal Reserve will be if it is maintaing steady NGDP growth and if it does, nothing is wrong (putting aside ethics scandals and the like). Carola Binder has also made the case that NGDP targeting is robust to populist pressures, and may even help long run growth further weaking the chance of a populist movement growing through robust growth. Also reflects questions of distribution and growth back to the politicians. There has always been a debate between ‘hawks’ and ‘doves’ and this would sidestep that debate entirely, and put the onus on politicians to do better fiscal and regulatory policy. This means structural reform to have higher production, higher growth means lower inflation (which is good for the incumbent party looking to be reelected), also potential to have less boom-bust cycles because finance is more secure and robust. It also really puts the onus on Congress to do things about inequality, investment, or favor certain sectors (or not), which is where those debates should be happening, not amongst unelected technocrats whose decisions affect millions of people and trillions of dollars. Speaking of which, stimulus won’t really work because it causes inflation which will eat into the growth and it is just redirecting spending from one thing to another. (This probably comes more from the Austrian view that stimulus doesn’t work, but I don’t think this is a very strong point.) A much stronger but related point is that bailouts likely won’t happen (as much) because that would be allocating a specified fraction of the economy to the bailout, which is likely to be unpopular because people would rather that money be used on something else or rather not bailout a failing business.

Covid and its aftermath really showed the importance of not tightening too much or too little when appropriate. I think it is just better policy when nominal incomes are maintained as it is really hard to know much supply shocks will feed into inflation and how quickly the supply shocks will fade, especially when there are geopolitical causes or random freak occurences, like the Ever Given getting stuck. NGDP targeting limits the growth of debt/credit in good times, and inflation is countercyclical. Most people and businesses think nominally anyway, especially in contracts. Finance would likely shift away from “safe assets” typically meaning government bonds. Also there should be less risk aversion and preference for straight cash because the nominal economy is growing at a predictable rate, so in slower times, more cash will become available via monetary policy, but not too much because the growth will be predictable. Predictable policy so people and businesses can plan long-term with less risks. Claudia Sahm’s idea of sending money to households can be the one final backstop to ensure nominal growth continues in the gravest of crises. It is also a more equitable way to increase growth and manage crises. However, the main areas progressive should embrace markets are housing, occupational licensing, health insurance, telecoms like EU, carbon pricing instead of income taxes, energy (depends on specifics), DBCFT and VAT instead of corporate tax, end subsidization of private retirement funding (401ks etc) and expand social security, tax cars more, do more ownership stakes in business when giving them startup funds, Georgism more and income taxes less, Green TVA? The Jones Act doesn't work anymore and doesn't achieve its goal of helping the US shipbuilding industry, and undermines other goals. Here is another article that says that repealing the Jones Act wouldn't completely devestate the industry because right now a lot of the work is done by foreign companies, and the US still makes good river barges. And that leads me to the point that it is kind of arbitrary what must be done in the US to qualify as domestic for purposes of the Jones Act. Ships can be designed abroad, several components can come from abroad, and the ship can still qualify as domestic. Meanwhile the whole point was to subsidize the shipbuilding industry so we could ramp up production with the latest and greatest technology, but who is to say that is the best way to maintain shipbuilding capacity with the best available technology? It certainly seems to me that in 2023 it is not. Maybe it would be better to fund vocational schools that teach people how to make ships well. Maybe it would be better to fund research directly on shipping technology. Maybe it would be better to regulate the ships used for certain emissions and safety standards, like we do for motor vehicles. Maybe we give tax credits if a percentage of a shipping company's cargo is carried on ships constructed in the United States, so that shipbuilders have a reason to become cost competitive with the rest of the world, but shipping companies can choose non-American ships in a way that is not as costly to consumers. We could also exempt remote locations, like the various island territories, Hawaii, Alaska, etc, from the Jones Act, or exempt certain goods, like oil, semiconductors and electronics, or food. I am just throwing ideas out there, but these seem better on the surface and deserve more exploration and research. the shipping industry. Also the dredging law and that Odd Lots episode. A brief history of the minimum wage

Nobel prize speech by Buchanan said people who think raising minimum wage doesn't increase unemployment is like believing that water flows uphill. The first minimum wage study that I know of is looking at restaurants on the border between New Jersey and Pennsylvania because New Jersey increased the minimum wage and Pennsylvania didn’t. This study is done by two famous economists David Card and Alan Krueger. They found that minimum wage just didn’t matter that much for employment or wages.

Particularly, they looked at the changes in restaurants and other low wage sectors that are most likely to be impacted by the increased minimum wage, and found little to no effect (maybe even a slight increase? But more on that later.) (Later research by Luca and Luca found that higher minimum wages seem to speed up failure of restaurant firms).

There was also research done in Seattle and it didn’t seem to matter that much at the beginning as they were ramping up to $15/hour but it did matter later. Lately, there has been more evidence that this is because of the labor market concentration that I mentioned earlier. Since Amazon is a large employer that pays its lower wage workers the same, they kind of act as minimum wages in the areas where they have large amounts of workers. This is also reinforcing some rural-urban economic divides because most people live in areas with large concentrations of employers. So raising the minimum wage is actually helpful IF wages are being held down to a significant degree, such that employers have to shell out a little more money for wages acting as a de-facto stimulus. Another thing I want to note is that some states have tied their minimum wage to inflation and others have not, including the federal government. There is a chart with the inflation adjusted minimum wage and the peak was basically the 1960’s. But then you start getting in to which inflation, and that is an entirely separate discussion. So the reason that this seems to be the case is that labor markets are highly concentrated, which means most people don't have a plethora of options of employers to work for in a given geographic area and skills. Unlike, say, cereal, or televesions, there aren't many options. In 2016, the White House Council of Economic Advisors wrote an issue brief on labor market concnetration and some of the labor market practices and laws that enhance labor monopsony. They also point out that with higher labor market concentration, the link between productivity and wages is lower. There is good evidence that this is the case from Azar, Ioanescu, and Steinbaum. There is also evidence from Amazon. When Amazon builds a warehouse in a new location and pays its workers at least $15 an hour, that sets a de facto minimum wage that other lower wage employers have to match to prevent workers from going to Amazon. There is also geographic monopsony for teachers because there isn't more than one school district to give teachers options. (Female dominance in teaching and motherhood probably affect this too, but that is a whole discussion.) In Seattle, a minimum wage increase reduced hours for some lower wage employees, but still raised earning for those whose wage increased because of the law. Real minimum wage peaked in 1968, according to CPI. Minimum wage increase in German lead to a reallocation of workers to larger, higher paying firms. It also only explained some of the total wage increase. CBO did a report on minimum wage at $10, $12, $15/hr and its effects on employment wages. It seems there are little effects at the beginning of the increases, but seems to see lower jobs five to ten years out. Noah Smith and Russ Roberts talked about minimum wage and they disagreed but highlighted monopsony and other labor and capital things in one of their episodes of Econtalk together. Arin Dube saying that there is so much data on minimum wage that if the true effect is ~0%, then there is lots of data finding positive and negative effects because there is a lot of noise. I also think the minimum wage literature is the go-to example in economics of publication bias.

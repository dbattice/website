Fiscal Federalism and Its Discontents

"I hate paying taxes. But I love the civilization they give me." - Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.

Taxes are the fundamental power of the state. States cannot do anything without tax revenue. Taxes seem to have developed with civilization. The Rosetta Stone is mostly a tax document. One of the primary functions of states historically was defense, funded by taxes. Hendrickson, Slater, and Albrecht have a paper on this, called Preventing Plunder. Macro Musing podcast with Hendrickson discusses this too. It is not a coincidence that the first article of the US Constitution gave the "power of purse" to the House of Representatives (more on that later), the most representative part of the federal government. Even taking one step back, part of the reason the Articles of Confederation failed was because the national government couldn't tax effectively, in addition to trade and treaty problems, and a toothless executive, highlighted by Shay's Rebellion.

The interesting thing to me is that most people do not live under one government; they live under multiple levels of government. The United States is a federal republic, meaning a federation with states (or regions) who have constitutional authority separate from the national government, and that all government officials are elected or chosen by elected politicians.

There are many reasons that federalism is desired, but it seems that it is one of three reasons as far as I can tell: ideological views that decentralization is just and the best way to do things (also called subsidiarity), decentralization allows heterogeneity and experimentation to adapt to local preferences (and by reducing political conflict by taking some issues off the table of national politics), and as a compromise between the central government and the regional governments. Unfortunately, I believe the reasons for federalism are at odds in the United States system of federalism because federalism as practiced today in the United States doesn't really fulfill any of these aims. Moreover, I think the root of many of the reasons federalism doesn't work as intended is partly because of taxation and spending policies, as practiced, and coordination problems between states. I don't see any reason you cannot make this better, but it would require significant federal legislation and a significant increase in state action, coupled with a shift in how Americans think about state government or state democracy. This does not necessarily mean constitutional changes, but that should be considered if you want to reinvigorate federalism. I will talk more about consitutional amendments later or in a separate article. Throughout this article, in the back of my mind, I am thinking about social insurance, marijuana, and the problem of housing and land use. I also grew up in a small town in a rural farming county with manufacturing and government employment anchoring the county, so I am also thinking about small towns where scale is just not a thing to take advantage of. At the same time, it is very beneficial to take advantage of large cities and the agglomeration effects they generate and are shaped by.

Since the United States is a federal republic, where states have a tremendous amount of power, and that taxation is one of the core features of a state (the abstract idea of a state, not subdivision of administration), it seems obvious that the taxing and spending powers of states are very important. But I also think that this more downstream of how state government works in practice today, from issues of constitutional design, to assumptions about state democracy being stuck in the past, and lack of imagination.

Institutions of Federalism

I was thinking about federalism because certain people like federalism but almost no one seems actually care across a variety of domains. Federalism is the system that states can do stuff and have their own sovreignty and legitimacy, regardless of national politics, only limited by the national and state constitutions. Even though I got interested in federalism as a way to solve major problems, this really is irrelevant to federalism as a core political philosophical commitment. The first thing to say is that for federalism to work properly, it must be democratic. In this vein, gerrymandering is extremely bad for state governments. I also strongly urge state governments to move to proportional representation. Open lists or mixed-member proportional representation is preferred. It doesn't really matter which because they are both easy to explain. (I know STV exists and might be better in theory, but I think it will be too complicated for too many people, creating unnecessary opposition to change or straight up fearmongering by opponents. I don't think this is actually a valid point because Australia and Ireland are just fine using STV. Also hard to do in a time of deep polarization and lower trust.) Also, unicameral state parliaments would be better than status quo of bicameral state legislatures (except Nebraksa) and separate elections for multiple state executive offices. (Even Italy, which separately elects its regional executive, still only elects the equivalent of the governor, which would be an improvement.)

So besides the dysfunctional in the twenty first century institutions, we also have a mess of fiscal powers. I think most Americans think America is very federal, but I would argue that the United States is not as decentralized (at least fiscally) as most Americans think. Switzerland or Belgium or Canada is probably closer to what people mean and want by federalism, but more on that later. So, if you want a decentralized government, you have to fund the subcentral governments, or at least allow the sub central governments to fund themselves. This can be done via subcentral governments collect their own taxes, giving them grants, or a mix of both. So let's look at taxes and grants in some other countries.

I find this interesting, but some of this is correlated with how much a country taxes in general. So let's look at subcentral taxes compared to central government taxes, using the OECD Tax Database. The central government measure includes social security contributions, which you could argue is just moving money around in the form of health insurance, pensions, etc. This is can be misleading because different countries fund these things with these contributions and others use general funds to fund the same things. So if you remove the social security contributions, you still end up with kind of the same result. There is also this helpful document from the OECD that tries to incorporate many different measures of subcentral government autonomy. This mostly lines up with what I expected, although Finland and the United Kingdom scored higher than I expected in some areas, and it doesn't include the United States.

So looking at this data, it seem clear to me if you actually cared about federalism, you should focus on more fiscal decentralization. Either slowly giving more taxation to states (and implicitly local governments), or more unconditional grants. This is not as easy as it sounds because of unequal fiscal capacities and crises that may arise.

Regional Inequality

In the United States, states have very different taxing capabilities. This means that some states will find it much easier to raise an addition $100 per capita, but also some states will have it easier to raise $1000000 because they have more people to spread that cost around onto. This, I think, is one thing that really needs to be improved if you support federalism. My prefered approach is two sets of grants, the first based on pure equalization, and the second based on need. This implies more money to poorer states, but probably more money for states that have more children in school, or more money for elderly services, for example, all else equal. There are some more things you can take into account, like how rural or urban states are, as in Switzerland.

I think it is worth providing context on why states should be equalized in the first place. To start with, different states have different economies with different sectors. For example, midwestern states have a lot of agriculture, Pennsylvania and North Dakota do a lot of fracking. Massachusetts has a large biomedical and education sector. To the extent the economy evolves over time, you would expect different sectors to become the new booming sector and other sectors of the economy to slump. Across the entire country, it should even out and in total we should get richer as the entire economy gets richer and evolves more towards the growth sectors. But this is bad for places disproportionally in the slumping sectors. So what should the federal government or state governments do? State governments can try to save money for a slumping time, but if the sectors they are most reliant on go into permenant decline this isn't going to help in the long run. So one thing the federal government can do is just general redistribution from richer states to poorer states, with unconditional grants based purely on whether the state has below average income. The other that it can do is facilitate retraining probably in cooperation with state or local governments. Beefing up unemployment insurance would also be a good way to help insure against recessions and facilitate structural change as needed. But state governments can also make it easier for people to move into their state by reducing occupational licensing laws and loosening land regulation to allow more housing construction. This does create additional problems though. For example, if people are moving with their children, then those states now have to spend more on schools in the short run, even though it will probably pay off in the long run. This is why it would be good to have need based grants as well. The flip side is that Florida has unusually many retirees, which means Florida is getting so much Social Security money. But any elderly services are going to disproportionally burden Florida. These are just two examples of needs that probably should be considered when designing grants to states.

The other big reason to have grants to states is that states experience idiosyncratic shocks or occasionally the entire country is in a recession. For idiosyncratic shocks, the same things for structural sectoral change apply. For generic recessions it would be helpful for the federal government to borrow billions of dollars and just give the money to states so they don't start cutting spending, meaning more layoffs and unemployed people in their state, which hurts state administrative capacity and makes the recession worse. This is kind of what happened after the 2008 crisis. Alex Williams also wrote about how states are assumed to be able to save for bad times, but the justifications are kind of weak because states don't really have that much control over the business cycle, and that is especially true when you consider the heterogenous composition of state economies and monetary policy being national. Also, the national politicians and state politicians benefit (or are harmed) differently from implementation of austerity. He proposed creating a fund that will pay out to states based on previous years' tax revenues as their unemployment rate rises. This is because state and local tax revenues are procyclical. The Tax Policy Center also has a similar idea even though it doesn't seem adequate enough to me and it is a better use of the SALT deduction than the status quo. Either way, these proposals mean states that tax more before a recession will get more money than those that tax less, so it doesn't change states's incentives to tax more or less, or give states a windfall, but still gives them a more stable budget over time so they can plan investments better. The United States could also adopt a VAT (more on this later) and use the some of the revenue to equalize state budgets, and in a recession give the states more revenue out of the total, like in Germany.

Related to recessions and borrowing is the fact that the United States government makes its own currency. This means it can implement monetary policy to affect its borrowing costs. States cannot do this. There is a whole literature in economics about whether the United States (or any country or set of countries really) about whether different places should have the same currency, called an optimal currency area. Robert Mundell was the main founder of this line of research. Basically the conditions for having the same currency include workers being able and willing to move from slumping areas to booming areas, allowing investments freely across the area with the same currency, allowing wages and/or prices to adjust as needed, built in insurance mechanisms to help subregions through idosyncratic shocks, and generally similar business cycles.

Principles of taxation and fiscal federalism

Now I get to talk about the real thing I wanted to talk about, which is fiscal federalism. This refers to how sub national governments finance their expenditures, but also includes which levels of government are doing what. Maybe the most important thing to say is that than direct taxation and spending is limited by the ability to set the terms of spending and who is supposed to do what. And this relationship of control is very hard to manage. You could just do everything with direct grants, which gives subcentral governments the power of spending (not talking about any conditions for the grants which reduces this power even further), but are still implicitly controlled by the higher levels of government. Or you could do the flip side and let every subnational government do what they want and have no involvement from the higher levels of government. There are reasons to think you want a mix, which I will get into in a moment. What I mean is that in other countries, (that I will talk about later), it is very clear who manages health spending, education, transportation, housing, and law enforcement, and this is key, how they are supposed to fund those things. Unfortunately, state and federal policy can work against each other (see Medicaid and marijuana), which I don't really think is federalism. I guess you could say the subnational government is doing what it wants, even if their constituents want it and there are bad incentives, but in that case it seems like you should reset the federal and regional powers, not work against each other. (For Medicaid specifically, you really would need to put health fully in the hands of states, like in Canada, or put it fully in the federal government, like in most other countries.) The tax system (and some spending) is not coordinated at all in the United States. Lastly, we don't try to equalize state wealth, which inherently makes federalism harder for some states, which I think undermines federalism for all states.

For tax policy, it is important to use sound taxation principles. The tax side should therefore be designed with federalism (or decentralization generally) in mind. See this for more on good taxation principles. Additionally, the Italian Finance Ministry says that subcentral governments are good at aligning to local preferences, they are more democratically accountable, have better incentives for growth, but you have to take into account spillovers, scale, and crises that hamper local governments. The OECD also has a nice report called Synthesising Good Practices in Fiscal Federalism. In this report they say there are three categories of recommendations. The first is to align subcentral taxation to subcentral spending via increasing tax autonomy and owns resource revenues, and increase equalization. The second is to clearly give certain repsonsiblities to each level of government (one policy, one sovereign principle). For example, Germany and Canada explicitly say in their constitutions what is and isn't subnational jurisdiction. The third category is to make coordination easier by removing internal barriers to market integration (zoning and occupational licensing for example), increase transparency on taxation and spending, coordinate procurement for subcentral governments, and create incentives to coordinate for certain policies that have large spillovers (positive or negative) or could result in different levels of government working against each other. This is related to the benefit principle, which says the level of government that will benefit from investments should be responsible for funding those investments. Related to the benefit principle is the concept of spillovers. (For example, if one state spends a lot of money on universities, it may be hard to capture the aggregate benefits from that spending, especially if the graduates are likely to leave the state. The reverse situation is if a state approves a factory or power plant whose pollution mostly flows over the border to a neighboring state. Given the high returns to university spending and the very high costs of pollution, we probably want to fund more universities and allow less polluting factories, but getting the legal and taxing powers right can be difficult.) Tax base mobility is also important because if you make lower levels of government dependent on mobile tax bases, like corporate taxes, you will heavily incentivize corporations to move to the lowest tax jurisdiction, and will recieve less overall revenue (look at Germany or Swizterland). This means that more mobile tax bases should be taxed by higher levels of government. The other issue to consider with tax bases is how much the tax base changes year to year. Consumption tax revenue and payroll tax revenue do not change much year to year, where as corporate tax revenue and alcohol taxes can change dramatically, year to year. This means we don't want local governments to be too reliant on one source of (volatile) tax revenue.

International comparisons

Because many countries face similar questions of how to assign taxation and spending to their different levels of government, I thought I would look at some other rich countries to see how they deal with these questions. There is a wide variety across countries, with some focusing more power in the central government, others, allow significant subcentral taxation and spending, and others rely mostly on grants, and almost every combiniation in between. Different policy areas are also handled by different levels of government, while some countries have more levels of government than others.

One case that is worth considering is Sweden. Sweden is not a federal country but is highly decentralized. In Sweden the main source of revenue for counties and municipalities is a flat income tax, with a combined average rate of 32%, although there is an earned income tax credit and deductions for seniors to lower the effective rate for low income indidvuals. Because people would prefer to pay less income taxes, the local governments are under pressure to use tax revenue very efficiently, both to lower taxes, but also draw in residents from nearby, growing the tax base. The national government levies payroll and social security taxes, a flat income tax for high income individuals, and a VAT and many excise taxes. On the spending side, regions mainly handle health care, some dental care, public transportation, and economic development. The municipalities handle everything from schools to social services and elderly care, to sanitation and child care and many other tasks.

Germany is a proper federation. Germany's constitution explicitly defines the federalism relations, by laying out tasks for the federal government, state governments, and which powers are shared. While Sweden has local governments that raise income taxes, Germany does a mix of revenue sharing, grants and own taxes. and the revenues of each are split in predetermined ratios between the federal government, state governments, and municipal governments. Most excise tax revenue remains with the federal government, but states recieve inheritance tax revenue and property transfer taxes. The value added tax revenue is one of the main equalizing mechanisms, along with grants for the three city-states, grants taking into account the tax revenues of the local governments, grants for the former East German States, and for for other reasons. There are also some cool things about local government financing, that I am not going to get into here. This complicated system of grants does three things. The first is that over eighty percent of taxes are the same no matter where you live, meaning states and local governments have to lure people and business on other dimensions, mostly preventing a race to the bottom on taxes. This system of grants also ensures that Germans have basically the same level of public goods and services no matter where they live. And thirdly, it give state and local governments incentives to grow their populations and do economic development, because they automatically get more tax revenue from population growth and by their residents getting richer.

Canada takes a different approach from Germany. The Canadian federal government has a progressive income tax, corporate tax, and value added tax (called a Goods and Services Tax). Instead of splitting the revenue like in Germany, the provinces levy their own taxes on top of the federal taxes, known as piggybacking, with the provinces deciding the corporate and income tax rates and brackets, and whether to have a value added tax or sales tax and how high of a rate. Canada also allows provinces to outsource most tax collection to the Canadian tax enforcement agency, the Canada Revenue Agency. Like Germany, Canada gives equalization grants to poorer provinces, but Canada also has two other funds called the Canadian Health Transfer,and the Canadian Social Transfer. These are both given to provinces on a per capita basis, are going to grow at least 3% for the next several years, meaning that provinces can plan around recieving these funds for several years. (The provinces can be penalized for inadequate social services, but have never been penalized for doing so. The penalities for the CHT have been applied, but were paid later for fixing the problems.) The CHT is to support the health systems of each province, while the CST support education, social services, elderly care etc. These two transfers were created by consolidating other grants and cost sharing programs, and funds have changed to be more flexible and to rely more on public accountability instead of federal government oversight. Although Belgium is quite decentralized on policy and spending, most taxation is still done centrally. This is complicated by the fact that Belgium has regions and communities. Short story on this is that communities are based around language, education, and "things dealing with the person", while regions are geographic areas, focused more on economic policy. Communities rely mostly on grants, but regions recieve grants and levy some taxes. Belgium is an interesting case of federalism because they only became a federation in 1993. This is mainly because they keep having government crises every decade or so since 1970, and the resolution is giving more authority to the regional or community governments. (Regions are based on geography, communities are based on language and includes education and other things.) Regions manage agriculture, regional development, transportation (not SNCB/NMBS), energy (nuclear energy is still federal), water policy, trade policy, management of local governments, and environmental policy. Communities manage arts, tourism, radio and television(including public broadcasters), language and education, libraries, some things related to health, youth, elderly, disability, and family policy. The national government manages the military, foreign affairs, the judicial system, social security and other welfare state policies, and the various federal institutions, like the Royal Military Academy, and state owned companies, like the National Railway Company of Belgium, and the things previously mentioned as federal government competencies. There are equalization payments to the poorer regions, and of course there are political tensions around this. and the VAT is used for communities and social security. This is information on taxes that go to regions and communities (and EU and social security) (sorry it is in French)) Subnational governments levy property taxes too In Belgium the federal government collects most tax revenue through income, payroll, VATs, and other taxes. The federal government has most pension, health, and social security costs. The federal government defines tax bases and the subnational governments controls more of property taxes and some other excise taxes.? Belgium has fiscally decentralized. The subnational governments collectly only levy about a quarter of their expenditures, mainly from a flat income tax on top of the nation income tax and some fees and tariffs. This is while subnational governments do about half of all government expenditure and employ about 80% of government employees. (The OECD thinks that Belgium should have more cooperation and better regulation across governments.

Switzerland definitely has a very federal government. While Canada gives large autonomy to provinces, Switzerland's cantons go even further. The national Swiss government levies a VAT that is only 7.7 percent, which is basically the lowest in Europe. The national government does levy corporate and income taxes, but not that much as a percent of GDP. The real action on taxation in Switzerland is cantonal and municipal. Both derive large amounts of revenue from income and corporate taxes. This has led to tax competition with low tax rates and efficient spending, or at least lower spending. (source) Even though they are very decentralized, the Swiss do have a national social security system, not too different from the United States, but much more comprehensive. ( Some cantons even have additional child benefits above the national ones.) They also do equalization, but asymmetrically; they focus on bringin up the poorest cantons to the average, while letting the richer cantons just pay some money into the equalizaiton scheme. This does seems like it would lower incentives for the poorer cantons to increase their wealth because they lose quite a bit from equalization as they get closer to average wealth. Most of the equalization is still done by the federal government giving money to poorer cantons. The federal government does give grants based on costs, notablely giving more money to city-cantons and mountainous cantons. Here is more (probably outdated) information on Swiss equalization. The cantons and municipalities do still have large incentives to increase their populations because they automatically get the tax revenue from new residents and corporations, not unlike Sweden. Because the cantons do their own thing, it is very hard to charactarize what the cantons (and municipalities) do of their own volition. Swiss cantons can even make treaties with other cantons and other countries! All cantons and some municipalities have their own police, education is almost exclusively cantonal except for the two federal universities, and the cantons set their own official language. Health care in Switzerland is considered one of the best and is somewhat managed at the national level, but also at the cantonal level, with municipalities doing some long term care too. There is a national railway company, and nationwide rail integration. It is usually considered the best or one of the best rail systems in the world, despite Swiss decentralization and the complexity required to build and maintain tracks, and coordinate all the schedules. Switzerland does really seem to be like what people think when they think about what federalism means.

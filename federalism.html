The United States is a federal republic, meaning a federation with states (or regions) who have constitutional authority separate from the national government, and that all government officials are elected or chosen by elected politicians. More generally, the reasons that people want federalism is one of three reasons as far as I can tell: ideological views that decentralization is just and the best way to do things (also called subsidiarity), decentralization allows heterogeneity and experimentation and leads to the best outcomes by allowing different policies in different places (and by reducing political conflict by taking some issues off the table of national politics), and as a compromise between the central government and the regional governments. Since the United States is a federal republic, where states have a tremendous amount of power, it seems obvious that the taxing and spending powers of states are very important. I believe the reasons for federalism are at odds in the United States system of federalism because federalism as practiced today in the United States doesn't really fulfill any of these aims. Moreover, I think the root of many of the reasons federalism doesn't work as intended is because of taxation and spending policies, as practiced. I don't see any reason you cannot make this better, but it would require significant federal legislation and a significant increase in state action. This does not necessarily mean constitutional changes, but that should be considered if you want to reinvigorate federalism. Because many countries face similar questions of how to assign taxation and spending to their different levels of government, I thought I would look at some other rich countries to see how they deal with these questions. There is a wide variety across countries, with some focusing more power in the central government, others, allow significant subcentral taxation and spending, and others rely mostly on grants, and almost every combiniation in between. Different policy areas are also handled by different levels of government, while some countries have more levels of government than others. One case that is worth considering is Sweden. Sweden is not a federal country but is highly decentralized. In Sweden the main source of revenue for counties and municipalities is a flat income tax, with a combined average rate of 32%, although there is an earned income tax credit and deductions for seniors to lower the effective rate for low income indidvuals. Because people would prefer to pay less income taxes, the local governments are under pressure to use tax revenue very efficiently, both to lower taxes, but also draw in residents from nearby. The national government levies payroll and social security taxes, a flat income tax for high income individuals, and a VAT and many excise taxes. On the spending side, regions mainly handle health care, some dental care, public transportation, and economic development. The municipalities handle everything from schools, social services, elderly care, sanitation, and child care and many other tasks. Germany is a proper federation. Germany's constitution explicitly defines the federalism relations, by laying out tasks for the federal government, state governments, and which powers are shared. While Sweden centers subnational government on local governments levies income taxes for their competences, Germany does a mix of revenue sharing, grants and own taxes. Value added taxes, corporate taxes, income taxes are the same everywhere in Germany, and the revenues of each are split in predetermined ratios between the federal government, state governments, and municipal governments. Most excise tax revenue remains with the federal government, but states recieve inheritance tax revenue and property transfer taxes. The value added tax revenue is one of the main equalizing mechanisms, along with grants for the three city-states, grants taking into account the tax revenues of the local governments, grants for the former East German States , and other among other reasons.

A Citizen's Guide to Taxation

Principles of taxation include tax things with negative externalities first. This is because taxing those things should lead to less of those things. It is has the additional feature of letting people consume what they want while making them pay more for consuming things with bad consequenes. This is called Pigouvian taxation. When governments levy excise taxes, the taxes revenue should be used to fund the solution to the costs you are trying to internalize. (There is an addtional reason, which is that excise tax revenue tends to be volatile and should not be relied on for general revenue.) This is why gasoline tax revenue is generally used to fund infrastructure, (see the Highway Trust Fund in the United States), and why cigarette taxes are used to fund the health system in some way, (at least in France and the United States). The next principle of taxation is to tax a broad base and at a low rate. This means that income taxes, corporate taxes, and sales taxes (or consumption taxes generally) should apply to all income, coprorate income, and all sales at the same rate. Unfortunately, many states tend to tax groceries at a lower rate or not at all. The best approach to consumption taxes is what New Zealand does because everything is taxed at 15%. (There is even a joke about when they legalized prostitution, someone asked if prostitution services would be subject to the VAT, and the finance minister said, 'Of course.') New Zealand has among the highest VAT revenues (OECD Tax Stats database code 5100).

The reason I mentioned New Zealand's VAT here is that it usually preferred to tax consumption instead of income or corporations. This is because savings are not taxed (more important in developing countries where they need savings to fund investments to grow richer), and it is much harder to evade taxes. Ultimately if you are taxed on income less, evenutally that money will be spent (yes, it is a little fuzzy for large nonprofit funds or generational fortunes) and taxed. Ramsey proved that you should tax inelastic goods and services more because it is more efficient. This is one reason taxing carbon would be good, because things that produce carbon are relatively inelastic. Diamond and Mirrlees showed that under some assumptions taxing final goods is the optimal way to tax consumption. There is also a paper on how under oligopolistic businesses, VATs raise more revenue than other taxes. And Worthwhile Canadian here is suggesting that you should tax consumption because it is more pro growth than other forms of taxation. There is more to this argument here, making the exact point I want to echo, that how to tax is not up for debate, but how much we should tax is. We have a pretty good idea that taxing consumption (sales tax) is better for the economy than taxing income or corporations. This is independent of how much tax revenue you want to raise. Lesson 6 here furthers this argument in the Swedish context. The main way this would work is probably lower administrative costs (and I suspect lower evasion too, with more on evasion later). It is usually preferable to tax capital income less than labor income, and labor income less than consumption. Correlary: payroll taxes are better than income taxes. I think this is especially true for 'welfare state' revenue. In other words, paryoll taxes in Finland or Sweden are ideal, because they do not have a cap and the revenue goes directly to the funding of pensions, health, unemployment insurance, parental benefits, sick pay, and anything else like that (minus the general payroll tax in Sweden, although that is a complex topic I don't want to deal with).

Income taxes should be lowered as consumption taxes rise, but the same principles of 'broad base, low rate' apply. I also think that pensions and labor income should be taxed the same, not special carveouts for seniors, or not taxing social security or whatever. if you want to help out seniors, just collect the tax revenue from the broad base and write checks to all people above retirement age. The same principle applies to families. Don't give special tax credits for families with children, tax all and write checks to parents every month, like a sane country. For people who are disabled or unemployed, same thing. Tax their income and unemployment payments as if it was income, and then make the payments universal. I know it seems a little redundant, but Matt Bruenig, Don Moynihan, Pamela Herd, and Annie Lowrey have written about administration of these payments, and why the cheaper, progressive thing to do is reduce administrative burdens on people, which in the US would probably be accomplished by making the Social Security Administration more comprehensive like Kela in Finland. The other thing is to make income taxes flatter, but individual instead of having individual, head of household, and married tax schedules. People seem to not like multiple tax brackets or think its unfair that people with higher incomes pay higher marginal tax rates, but I just don't think it matters that much. Across all the OECD countries, there is a great deal of variation in income tax progressivity (and income tax revenue as a percent of GDP and total tax revenue) and it just is not obvious it has a consistent clear effect either way. The real way to reduce income tax complexity is to have the income tax have many fewer dedcutions and credits, and also have the IRS do pre-filled tax returns. This is basically where the IRS takes the information it already has and says "hey, does this look correct?" If it does, then you respond that it is correct and you are done. If not, you do what you do now for income taxes and start going through the long forms.

I am also quite confident in saying that DBCFT would be better than the current US corporate tax, although it could be quite devastating for smaller and developing countries. This can get more complicated when taking into account other priorities. (how progressive should income taxation be? how much should corporations be taxed? These I think are more political than economic because of evidence from the OECD having lots of variation. There are just questions that matter way more than tweaking the corporate tax rate or income tax brackets slightly.)

You might notice that I have not mentioned property taxes yet. This is because I think everyone should move to land value taxes (used, barely, by Estonia, Denmark, and Taiwan) and massively upzone land (closer to Japan), and this also means that inputed rents should be taxed. And remembering from earlier about how inelastic things should be taxed more, land is the most inelastic thing there is (minus the Netherlands of course) and is impossible to evade. One important thing to keep in mind is that you shouldn't favor home ownership in tax policy, because among other things, not favoring home ownership should lead to a more dynamic economy as people are less tied to a specific place and are more likely to move to better opportunities. (There are also problems with things like mortgage intereset deduction, SALT deduction, and not taxing gains from houses if you immediately buy another house with the funds, just to name a few. I could right several posts about this, but I will stop here for now.)

The other thing I want to mention is distributional issues. People are rightly concerned about who pays taxes and if some people are not paying their fair share. So consumption taxes do tend to be regressive, but this can be offset with generic tax credits. Income taxes can be flat or progressive, and usually have a threshold before you start paying income taxes. Corporate taxes have no obvious implications, other than don't make them too high because it seems bad for growth and investment. (pretty sure there are papers I can sight here) Property taxes tax home owners/land owners, but there are so many loopholes it is not even really a proper property tax. A great way to increase property tax revenue would be reduce the loopholes. (Again, I could write a whole post on failures of property taxes, but that is for another time.) The main thing with corporate, income, and property taxes is to reduce the number of exceptions. It tends to be the largest corporations and the richest people who use the exceptions the most, and they often lobby to include or keep these exemptions that only a few people benefit from, at the cost of higher taxes on everyone else.

Tax evasion is the other big thing that citizens should think about. You really want to have broad based consumption taxes as a backstop on other forms of evasion. Also, Henrik Kleven has a nice paper saying that Denmark can have high taxes because evasion is low because most taxes are reported by third parties. There is further evidence that if income tax evasion is high, it is better to have higher consumption taxes. The logic of this is that higher income taxes lead to more evasion, so then you have lower and flatter income taxes (which results in lower revenues), so if you want more revenue, raise the consumption tax rate. For most Americans, it is basically impossible to evade property taxes, sales taxes and income, social security, and Medicare taxes employees who are not self employed in the United States, but is even more true in Denmark because of how VATs work. Even my bank tells the IRS that if I make sufficient interest in my bank account, not mention the bank has to report my banking information if the government gets a warrant. (Although the real champ here is the Faroe Islands with honorable mention to Estonia.) Also, I can't help but mention the story of the Danish King Christian IV as a great example of tax enforcement. One final pendatic, but probably the most political in this entire post, is that optimal revenue will not be perfect enforcement. What I mean is that tax enforcement costs money, and given that no tax enforcement raises no money and perfect tax enforcement would have a very high cost for that last thousand dollars of tax revenue the optimal point on strictly net revenue grounds (net revenue=total revenue collected-enforcement costs) will be below the revenue collected from point of 100% tax compliance. Note I am not talking about all the random deductions and credits in the tax code that make it super complex, strictly tax evasion, although the complexity makes it easier to evade by making it harder to ensure some paid the correct taxes and by making it easier to shuffle things to make it look legimate because one could be using the dedcutions and credits legitimately. I think this is very bad because it undermines confidence in the fairness of taxation, and reduces the willingness to raise taxes necessary to fund public goods and maintain high trust that no one is cheating the system. Basically, don't become Italy, become Faroe Islands (with your preferred tax rates as you please).

Now, when here your state or municipality is considering raising taxes, the only thing you should be asking is 'is the revenue going to be spent on something I like or not?' People will say dumb things about taxes, because they don't like taxes. But some spending is quite popular, like paying teachers more, or funding local libraries. (Melinda Cooper wrote a paper on Neoliberalism and the Virginia School, where one of the points she makes is that people think the government should spend more on various policy areas but don’t like higher taxes. And this seems corroborated by Saez finding that tax increases are more likely to pass by referendum or ballot initiative if tied to particular thing.) But even this misses some of the main questions, like is this an efficient way to accomplish the goal? Sometimes there is no other way, in the case of funding libraries, for example, you just have to hire people and pay for books to have more books in the library. But in the case of a city bus network, you could also consider cutting (or reducing the frequency of) the least used routes, buying more fuel efficient buses to save money for other purposes. The point is that putting more money into something isn't always the most efficient way to accomplish a goal, even if it is the politically favored way. (There are a couple of things I don't really want to mention here because I think they are bad policy, but you could consider cutting some government employee salaries, which I would only do for relatively unskilled jobs that are clearly way overpaid relative to the private sector, or getting higher levels of government to fund your thing, which encourages overspending and lack of trying to minimize unnecessary costs. This is somewhat politically defined, but Alon Levy has documented many ways in which transit is too expensive. One example is that Amtrak paid way too much money for new high speed trains.)

To further illustrate this point, I think it is helpful to look at examples. Various countries do various things for climate reasons. The straightforward thing to do is tax carbon dioxide emissions, but that runs into many political problems. So, one thing that you can do instead is mandate efficiency on appliances or vehicles, require a percentage of electricity come from green sources, or you can do backdoor taxes and subsidies. It seems that the best thing for poorer households though is the carbon tax. However, there was a very interesting paper published by the Norwegian statistics agency saying that businesses preferred the regulatory approach because it didn't increase taxes. More generally, the Niskanen Center published a piece about how regulations can increase costs even though the regulations are supposed to decrease costs or increase quality, but these costs are largely paid by government agencies, meaning the regulations are increasing expenditure which is covered by more taxes. Going back to the private sector, many benefits form working are funded by broad payroll taxes in other countries, but in the US they are often mandated if they even exist. The obvious example here is health insurance, but sick pay also falls in this category. (I do find it hilarious that China is forcing capitalists to donate), money instead of taxing them.) To further illustrate this point, I want to compare taxes and spending in Australia, and the United States. Most of the big spending categories in rich countries is pensions and health. So in Australia and the government collects a bunch of tax revenue and spending it directly on insurance or public hospitals, where as in the United States, it is a mix of taxes and mandated spending by businesses. This has nothing to do with how much each country spends on health, but if the United States was to spend all of its money via the government instead of roughly half and half that would immediately increase government taxes and spending. For pensions, Australia writes everyone one over a certain age a check and then mandates business put money into something like a 401(k), that workers can withdraw from in retirement. The United Kingdom seems to have a basic social security scheme like the United States, but also an occupational pension that people have to opt out of if they want. The United States has its Social Security scheme and 401(k)s and other savings schemes. So on paper, Australia spends the least on pensions, however, their retirement accounts have a contribution rate higher than the employer portion of social security in the United States. Is Australia's pension scheme really cheaper? They are just funding without the government directly taxing and spending, but the government is mandating spending, which functions like a tax. Matt Bruenig has a great post on how if you look at the health system the same way, the US actually has the second highest taxes as a percent of compensation. (Side note: it seems like in the United States today, there could be a compromise where richer people and businesses agree to pay more taxes in exchange for fewer regulations, but devil is in the details, I am not sure if businesses would take that deal.) The arguments we should have about taxation is how we fund specific things like transportation (should roads be funded with car taxes and fuel taxes, or general revenue?) or sales taxes vs income taxes vs property taxes. The other thing is that due to Baumol's cost disease and Wagner's law, taxes tend to go up over time and likely will in the future especially because the government funds most of pensions, health care, and education and the population is getting older in almost every country. I really wish the people that didn't like taxes would focus more on corruption and private incentives, and the win-win oppportunities of lowering of government spending while still achieving goals, like the aforementioned example of Amtrak overpaying for new trains.

A couple of other odds and ends: laws that limit tax rates are dumb. If you (the public via the government) want to spend money on something, you raise the taxes to do so. That is literally it. What matters is what household can actually buy and if you are making good investments in public projects. After all, Adam Smith said that purpose of production is consumption. I am very skeptical of automatic rebates or lowering of taxes as well. Referendums on taxes have problems because they can be captured by special interests and focus the debate on dumb things. People don't understand finance, so be a little skeptical of claims involving bonds, debt, and deficits. Which reminds me that there are times when deficits are required for a good economy, but this in recessions and should be done by the federal government (or the national government of your country). I saw a joke on how Sweden has had no net increase in debt for about 20 years; what an amazingly irresponsible fiscal policy (which was accompanied by negative bond rates for a lot of the post 2008 recession period).

How I would design the tax system Taking the insights from above, I would tax pollution and fossil fuels to the max, including carbon tariffs. Also, alcohol seems very bad, and alcohol consumption skews to richer people so tax alcohol more. Sugar also seems bad, so I would tax it more. I have seen something before on how sugar is produced by a literal single digit number of families, and the olipoly keeps enriching them. Matt Yglesias suggested that we do an excise tax on sugar and lower sugar tariffs. This would help Latin America develop and make sugar more expensive, and cut into oligopolistic rents of domestic sugar producers. Almost certainly this would get more revenue than now too. After doing those excise taxes, I would then implement a land value tax. I would then apply a DBCFT. Then apply a broad base VAT like New Zealand. After that, social security taxes to fund public pensions, unemployment insurance, paid family/sick leave, and accident and disability insurance. I am open to doing something like Luxembourg or the Netherlands where employers contribute a small percent of wages to fund accident and disbility insurance, which wouldn't exactly count as a tax, but might as well be, like the example of Australia's pensions earlier. After all of that, then income taxes matter. Probably can just set the threshold high enough that only ten to thirty percent of people even pay income taxes and sure, go ahead and make it a flat income tax as a concession to right wingers who abhor progressive income taxes for some reason. This income tax would probably be 30% or less and still have a broad and inclusive base. So far, this is has been generic for any government.

A Citizen's Guide to Taxation

Principles of taxation include tax things with negative externalities first. This is because taxing those things should lead to less of those things. It is has the additional feature of letting people consume what they want while making them pay more for consuming things with bad consequenes. This is called Pigouvian taxation. When governments levy excise taxes, the taxes revenue should be used to fund the cost you are trying to internailze. (There is an addtional reason, which is that excise tax revenue tends to be volatile and should not be relied on for general revenue.) This is why gasoline tax revenue is generally used to fund infrastructure, (see the Highway Trust Fund in the United States), and why cigarette taxes are used to fund the health system in some way, at least in (France and the United States.) The next principle of taxation is to tax a broad base and at a low rate. This means that income taxes, corporate taxes, and sales taxes (or consumption taxes generally) should apply to all items at the same rate. Unfortunately, many states tend to tax groceries at a lower rate or not at all. The best approach to consumption taxes is what New Zealand does because everything is taxed at 15%. (There is even a joke about when they legalized prostitution, someone asked if prostitutions services would be subject to the VAT, and they finance minister said, 'Of course.') New Zealand has among the highest VAT revenues (OECD Tax Stats database code 5100). The reason I mentioned New Zealand's VAT here is that it usually preferred to tax consumption instead of income or corporations. This is because savings are not taxed (more important in developing countries where they need investment to grow richer), and it is much harder to evade taxes. Ultimately if you are taxed on income less, evenutally that money will be spent (yes, it is a little fuzzy for large nonprofit funds or generational fortunes) and taxed. Ramsey proved that you should tax inelastic things more because it is more efficient. This is one reason taxing carbon would be good, because things that produce carbon are relatively inelastic. Diamond and Mirrlees showed that under some assumptions taxing final goods is the optimal way to tax consumption. There is also a paper on how under oligopolistic businesses, VATs raise more revenue than other taxes. And Worthwhile Canadian here is suggesting that you should tax consumption because it is more pro growth than other forms of taxation. The main way this would work is probably lower administrative costs (and I suspect lower evasion too, with more on evasion later). It is usually preferable to tax capital income less than labor income, and labor income less than consumption. Correlary: payroll taxes are better than income taxes. I think this is especially true for 'welfare state' revenue. In other words, paryoll taxes in Finland or Sweden are ideal, because they do not have a cap and the revenue goes directly to the funding of pensions, health, unemployment insurance, parental benefits, sick pay, and anything else like that (minus the general payroll tax in Sweden, although that is a complex topic I don't want to deal with). I am also quite confident in saying that DBCFT would be better than the current US corporate tax, although it could be quite devestating for smaller and developing countries. This can get more complicated when taking into account other priorities. (how progressive should income taxation be? how much should corporations be taxed? These I think are more political than economic because of evidence from the OECD having lots of variation. There are just questions that matter way more than tweaking the corporate tax rate or income tax brackets slightly.)

You might notice that I have not mentioned property taxes yet. This is because I think everyone should move to land value taxes (used barely by Estonia Denmark and Taiwan) and massively upzone land (closer to Japan). and this also means that inputed rents should be taxes. Basically, you shouldn't favor home ownership in tax policy. (There are also things like mortgage intereset deduction, SALT deduction, and not taxing gains from houses if you immediately buy another house with the funds, just to name a few.) I could right several posts about this, but I will stop here for now.

The other thing I want to mention is distributional issues. People are rightly concerned about who pays taxes and if some people are not paying their fair share. So consumption taxes do tend to be regressive, but this can be offset with generic tax credits. Income taxes can be flat or progressive, and usually have a threshold before you start paying income taxes. Corporate taxes have no obvious implications, other than don't make them too high because it seems bad for growth and investment. (pretty sure there are papers I can sight here) Property taxes tax home owners, but there are so many loopholes it is not even really a proper property tax. A great way to increase property tax revenue would be reduce the loopholes. (Again, I could write a whole post on failures of property taxes, but that is for another time.) The main thing with corporate, income, and property taxes is to reduce the number of exceptions. It tends to be the largest corporations and the richest people who use the exceptions the most, and they often lobby to include or keep these exemptions that only a few people benefit from, at the cost of higher taxes on everyone else.

Tax evasion is the other big thing that citizens should think about. You really want to have broad based consumption taxes as a backstop on other forms of evasion. Also, Henrik Kleven has a nice paper saying that Denmark can have high taxes because evasion is low because most taxes are reported by third parties. This is mostly true for sales taxes and most employees who are not self employed in the United States, but is even more true in Denmark because of how VATs work. (Although the real champ here is the with honorable mention to .) (Also, the story of the Danish King Christian IV is a great example of tax enforcement. One final pendatic, but probably the most political in this entire post, is that optimal revenue will not be perfect enforcement. What I mean is that tax enforcement costs money, and given that no tax enforcement raises no money and perfect tax enforcement would have a very high cost for that last thousand dollars of tax revenue the optimal point on strictly net revenue grounds (net revenue=total revenue collected-enforcement costs) will be below the revenue collected from point of 100% tax compliance. Note I am not talking about all the random deductions and credits in the tax code that make it super complex, strictly tax evasion, although the complexity makes it easier to evade by making it harder to ensure some paid the correct taxes and by making it easier to shuffle things to make it look legimate because one could be using the dedcutions and credits legitimately. I think this is very bad because it undermines confidence in the fairness of taxation, and reduces the willingness to raise taxes necessary to fund public goods and maintain high trust that no one is cheating the system. Basically, don't become Italy, become Faroe Islands (with your preferred tax rates as you please).

Now, when here your state or municipality is considering raising taxes, the only thing you should be asking is 'is the revenue going to be spent on something I like or not?' People will saying dumb things about taxes, because they don't like taxes. But some spending is quite popular, like paying teachers more, or funding local libraries. But even this misses some of the main questions, like is this an efficient way to accomplish the goal? Sometimes there is no other way, in the case of funding libraries, for example, you just have to hire people and pay for books to have more books in the library. But in the case of a city bus network, you could also consider cutting (or reducing the frequency of) the least used routes, buying more fuel efficient buses to save money for other purposes. The point is that putting more money into something isn't always the most efficient way to accomplish a goal, even if it is the politically favored way. (There are a couple of things I don't really want to mention here because I think they are bad policy, but you could consider cutting some government employee salaries, which I would only do for relatively unskilled jobs that are clearly way overpaid relative to the private sector, or getting higher levels of government to fund your thing, which encourages overspending and lack of trying to minimize unnecessary costs. This is somewhat politically defined, but Alon Levy has documented many ways in which transit is too expensive. One example is that Amtrak paid way too much money for new high speed trains.) A couple of other odds and ends: laws that limit tax rates are dumb. If you (the public via the government) want to spend money on something, you raise the taxes to do so. That is literally it. I am very skeptical of automatic rebates or lowering of taxes as well. Referendums have problems because they can be captured by special interests and focus the debate on dumb things. People don't understand finance, so be a little skeptical of claims involving bonds, debt, and deficits. Which reminds me that there are times when deficits are required for a good economy, but this in recessions and should be done by the federal government (or the national government of your country.) I saw a joke on how Sweden has had no net increase in debt for about 20 years; what an amazingly irresponsible fiscal policy (which was accompanied by negative bond rates for a lot of the post 2008 recession period).

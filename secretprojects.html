Notes on surveys, cognitive biases, and politics

Why I don’t like surveys, inflation expectations, Michigan Survey and partisanship, Fed research finding that people drastically overestimate inflation, going back decades; paper from Bank of Canada by Song and MacCauley about how twitter drives some of people’s pessimism about the economy. Twitter thread about this. I cannot believe people misunderstand supply restrictions on housing this badly. They really are dumb unfortunately. Joseph Ucinsky and Michael Schermer on conspiracies. Conspiracies happen when you feel powerless. Internet made it easy to connect with other conspiracists. Conspiracies are constant but manifest differently over time. French believe moon landings were fake. Out of power party gets more conspiratorial about party in power. Conspiracy theories are kind of a comfort when you don’t have control. Collective suffering lessens psychological problems, like Londoners getting bombed in WWII. Also COVID? Pluralistic ignorance, people hate opposite partisans for views they don’t really hold, politicians don't really know how rich or poor their constiutents are but it probably wouldn't change much even if they did, people wildly misperceive demographics of the other party, people underestimate inequality, etc. People with similar beliefs become more extreme when they are around people who are similar to them. This reminds me of positive feedback in a closed system, in which a closed system with only positive feedback is inherently unstable and will have larger and larger oscillations. It seems bad to have a political system with highly clustered ideologies and single member districts, but I guess that leads to wanting multimember districts, proportional representation, and multiple parties.

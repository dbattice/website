Why Canada should annex the United States

More people make you stronger geopolitically. America is a geopolitical heavyweight due to its large population and high incomes. Growing our population would give us more clout. Integrating more countries together would further science and development. Additionally this is an excuse to talk about devolution and parliamentarism. Canada has parliamentarism and decentralization of immigration, welfare, education, and health. Provincial government is better than state government because of parliamentarism and unicameralism, plus independent redistricting commissions. Taxation is about fifty fifty between the federal and provincial governments, minus social security. (See Switzerland for how to do social security in a highly decentralized country.) Courts are not as important. Canada has lots of hydro power. The US can build fracking in Canada mitigating problems with Russian gas, climate change, and related issues. Canada gives media regulation to provinces (radio especially). I increasingly think that Canada should annex the states one by one and the territories too. I first saw this as a joke about how the US should annex Canada, but I think the reverse should happen. The only criticism I have is that the municipal government isn’t the best, but still probably better because it is more centralized in the largest municipality in a metro area.

Maximum Canada by Doug Saunders is a similar idea and Noah Smith thinks it is happening. He also points out that Canada should probably do this so they can resist crazy America if it comes to that. Misha Saul also proposed to let Hong Kongers make their own city in Australia.

In addtion to this annexation, the combined country should consider annexing (at mutual agreement) a slew of other territories that have in the past wanted to be part of Canada or the United States. The one that is most controversial is Taiwan. This would immediately stop Chinese flaunting of its military near Taiwan, immediately help the US position on semiconductors and technology. It would give more Americans a back door to better urbanism, or at least examples of such. This could backfire as China gets even more paranoid and aggressive. But if we make it clear we are not aggressive, they have no leg to stand on. These options also apply to Hong Kong and Macau and Singapore, but for different economic reasons, and not as strategic geopolitically. These countries all have something else to contribute institutionally and culturally, while expanding geopolitical weight of Mega Canada. Canada has previously talked about letting in Turks and Caicos, St.Pierra and Miquelon, Bermuda, Bahamas, and Barbados. Iceland has also previously discussed joining Canada. These would be fine additions with lots of raw materials, tourism, and more water based geopolitical power. The United States has discussed adding Greenland, the Mexican states of Yucatán, Quintana Roo, and Campeche (which is basically the Yucután peninsula), the Azores (now part of Portugal but an important point during the Cold War), Canary Islands (same), Baja California, and Baja California Sur (so close to California already).

Starting notes for urbanism

M Nolan Gray wrote a fantastic book about zoning called Arbitrary Lines. In this book, he walks through the history of zoning in the United States and thinks it has utterly failed. He proposes an alternative, which is the starting point of how to fix zoning below. Abolish parking requirements, maximum heights, setback requirements, FAR limits, coverage ratios, and minimum lot sizes. Parking requirements are bad as Donald Shoup (and others) have shown. Floor to area ratio (FAR) is the ratio of inside floor area to the area of the land the building sits on. More floors means higher FAR. Larger building on less land means higher FAR. Michael Eliason pointed out how a high FAR can be bad, and that we allow too much building on the edges of urban areas. He thinks that the sweet spot is mid rise building with FAR under 5. This doesn't quite make sense to me because many cities have tall compact buildings next to each other, although maybe that is special because it is commercial real estate in central business districts? Michael Elias gives an example of a 29 story wood building with a FAR of about 5. I think FAR is more about personal preferences and lot sizes and ease of splitting parcels or joing adjecent parcels. Coverage ratio is the percent of the parcel of land that has a building on it. This ignores multiple stories. More on this later, but minimum lot sizes are indeed restrictive. A paper by Gyorko and McCulloch shows that minimum lot sizes increase home size, but reduce density. Many good recommendations here.

Do transit oriented development around rail stations and frequent bus routes/hubs. Reregulate automobiles, increase gasoline taxes and car registration fees and toll roads.

Good examples include Berlin, Freiburg im Breisgau, Munich, Vienna, Zurich, Basel, Milan, Paris, Strasbourg, Rennes, Lund is a university town on a commuter rail line close to Malmö. Stockholm transit oriented development challenging geography for metro high commuter rail ridership too biking in not-winter, Helsinki has great housing goals good transit, and very walkable. Amsterdam is Amsterdam, Utrecht is a university town with trams and the busiest rail station in Netherlands and an intercity rail hub, Almere is a recent construction on an island reclaimed from sea, Delft is decent size city with trams and trains between Rotterdam and Den Haag, Groningen innovative city smallish with lots of students, Ghent history on car free center and politics there of. Dordrecht smaller towns connected to bigger city, near nature, historical Roosendaal seems to be commuter town with cross border trains Seville biking metro walkable center outer areas are good too pedestrianized avenida de la constitución, Madrid metro is great, Barcelona metro is great improving biking, Pontevedra small city car free center people walk a lot, Prague eastern europe metro and commuter rail horrible for cars though, Brno has super high tram ridership, London innovative on fares, large city with good transit, New York City extensive 24 hour metro plus buses and biking very walkable, Toronto decent subway improving the GO transit to be an S-Bahn does have density in center but politics screw up the city, Auckland upzoning and transit, Vancouver very frequent metro can be expanded walkable city, Tokyo is Tokyo, Seoul cheap construction very good subway, Hong Kong has a private, very profitable metro.

There are no excuses for doing urbanism well. See Oulu, Helsinki, Trondheim, Seville, Santiago, Copenhagen, Munich, Tokyo, Lisbon, Amsterdam, Pontevedra, Houten, Zermatt, Berlin, and Rome.

All of this is wrong. Oulu and Helsinki are cold and yet Oulu is pretty good for biking, including complex contracts to clear bike paths quickly when it snows. Helsinki isn't quite as cold, isn't quite as good for cycling, but still has lots of walking and transit. On the other end of the spectrum, Seville Spain is notorious for being one of the hottest cities in Europe. And yet it is probably the cycling leader in Spain, has a good bus network, has a commuter rail network, one metro line (for now), and is quite walkable. They even turned one of the busiest roads in the middle of the downtown into a pedestrian street. They do have adaptions to the heat, like putting up cover over the busiest pedestrian streets in the summer. The other key point is that Santiago is not nearly as rich as any city in the United States, Europe, Asia, or Oceania with good urbanism. Starting notes for Spanish history

Iberia was settled by many different people, including groups the Celts, Aquitanians, Basques, Lusitanians, Tartessians, Gallaecans. Ibera was partly conquered by the Carthaginians. Rome then destroyed Carthage and took control of its empire. Along the way, Tiberius Gracchus negotiated a treaty with the Numantians (now in the province of Soria, Castille and Leon). Tiberius Gracchus was deemed to have done badly with this treaty, even though he saved Romans from being slaughtered. This was one of the earliest events in his life that he is known for, and Tiberius Gracchus was one of the key figures in the story of how Rome ended up weak enough to become an empire, with Julius Caesar being the first emperor. (The Numantians still live on in Spain, for example the name of the soccer club CD Numancia.) Rome collapsed (slowly) and then the Goths took over the Iberian Peninsula. (One famous remnant of the Visigoths today in Spain is the Mosque-Cathedral of Córdoba (Mezquita-catedral de Córdoba). The Visigoths built a church in the city of Córdoba, and then later, Muslims built a mosque over it.) In the 700s the Muslims conqured almost all of Iberia (minus Asturias), would have continued but were beaten by Charles Martel in the Battle of Tours. (There seems to have been a civil war in Spain probably related to struggle for the throne. And some good old fashioned opportunism by the Muslims.) The Muslims established a capital of their Iberian territory in Cordoba. Fast forwarding a few centuries, Isabel of Castile and Fernando II married. Isabel faced a challenge to her rule, and Fernando helped secure the throne? The marriage was definitely political, as it secrued Isabel on the throne of Castilla. Also, they are called the Reyes Catolicos, or Catholic Monarchs, but Isabel was absolutely pious, and Fernando was Catholic as a convenience, for political reasons more than true piety. The Reconquista started shortly after the conquest of Iberia, only ending in 1492, when Fernando and Isabel finally conquered Granada (and eventually were buried there). (The final phase of the war is called the Granada War, and involves betrayal, corruption, and a siege.) They went on to sponsor Christopher Columbus's voyage to America. (Patrick Wyman says that Columbus is the most replaceable figure in history.) Fernando and Isabel had a daughter Juana la Loca who married Felipe el Hermoso, son of the Holy Roman Emperor, and they had a son Charles V. Isabel died in 1504, at which point, Juana la Loca nominally ruled but was sequestered in a castle in Tordesillas while Fernando maintained the Castillian kingdom. (Not actually crazy, probably, but how can anyone maintain perfectly good health while being secuestered so long?) Meanwhile, Fernando wanted a son, and married a French noblewomen, Germaine de Foix. The attempt to have a son failed, but the throne of part of Navarra was tied to Fernando and his heirs, and the other part became part of the French Kingdom. When Felipe el Hermoso died a couple months after Fernando II, Juana ruled jointly with her son Charles V, although Charles continued to policy of keeping his mother locked away in Tordesillas. Charles became the king of almost everywhere except most of modern France and the British Isles. Charles inherited the Spanish territories in the Americas, and in the Pacific, not to mention most of the Iberian Peninsula, and had family connection to Portugal (which included Brazil and places like Taiwain and Macau) many islands in the Mediterranean, and southern Italy. And since Felipe el Hermoso was the son of Maxilimilian I and Mary of Burgundy, Charles V inherited a chunk on land in what is now Eastern France. Burgundy was also possibly the richest place in Europe at the time, such that his mother was often called 'the Rich'. He also conquered the Duchy of Milan. Once his grandfather Maximilian I died, the Holy Roman Empire was up for grabs. At the time, the position was chosen by seven electors, who were noblemen with other titles and lands. And by chosen, I mean bribed. Charles did indeed pay off the electors, using money borrowed by the Castillian kingdom, which put him in massive debt. (This was part of the process that lead to European finance, and in this case Charles borrowed money from Jakob Fugger to finance the Imperial throne.) It should be noted that the Holy Roman Emperor didn't actually have that much power, but he had a huge base of allies he could call on (theoretically, probably had to negotiate somewhat.) Charles was very successful at expanding territory. He also was a huge defender of Catholicism, including being the one who Martin Luther argued with and fled from later, and also fought off the Ottomans, including the famous Battle of Lepanto. In 1556, Charles was tired with life, not helped by his poor health from inbreeding, and retired to Extremadura Spain. He gave all his lands and titles to his son Felipe, except the Holy Roman Empire, which he gave to his brother Ferdinand II. Charles had a son Felipe II, who moved the capital of Spain from Toledo to Madrid because it was more central. In the 1600s, Felipe's descendents were getting so much money from the gold and silver from the Americas, which went up the Guadalquivier through Cadiz and Seville. Spain also had the best soldiers in the 1500s through middle of 1600s, the tercios. Spain was one of the princple nations involved in the Thirty Years War, and towards the end lost military dominance, lost money from Americas, lost land to France and others, Conde-Duque de Olivares was corrupt and bad, Catalonia and Portugal revolted, and Spanish elites prioritized keep Catalonia because they didn't want France and Catalonia allying, and thus lost Portugal forever. Also treaties with France and Pigeon Island. At the start of the 1700s, Carlos II didn't have an heir because of inbreeding, which triggered a war of succcession. This is when Spain became united as Spain due to the Nueva Planta decrees. This meant Spanish, French, and Italian monarchs couldn't have more than one of those thrones. This matters later. Brief Spanish republic, followed by the restoration and Alfonso XII and his son, Alfonso XIII. Also start of industrialization. Also the Basque Fueros, and centralization of Spain. In the 1800s there was the liberal revolutions and Isabel II. WWI followed by republicanism and dictatorships and turbulent political times, abdication of Alfonso XIII, Miguel Primo de Rivera and successors, fighting in Morocco, Civil Wars and Franco Dictatorship. Restoration of (weak) monrachy, Juan Carlos I and Adolfo Suarez and transition to democracy. Booming economy, becoming a typical western european country. 2008 crash and then catalan unrest, and Covid, through 2023 elections.

Starting notes for airports and air travel and air cargo transport

Berlin Brandenburg Airport and the absolute failure. CGP Grey on airport runway numbers, very interesting and of course there is an international dispute about how to handle the moving magnetic North Pole. Air traffic control uses a modified form of polar coordinates. There is a thing in commercial air travel called flying standby. This is basically a way for airlines to milk out a few more dollars from a few passengers when the plane otherwise wouldn't be full. If you are going somewhere and have a very flexible schedule, you can fly standby, where you just wait to make sure that the plane going to your destination will not be full, and then you fly cheaper in a random seat. The downside is you cannot rely on this in case a normal customer comes and does book a ticket in advance. This mostly seems to be a thing for people who work or know someone who works in the airline industry. Airport security is not what you think. In America, the TSA runs airport security, which was created by nationalizing the industry after 9/11. It is not clear whether the TSA is actually effective since the agents can pretty routinely smuggle things through when secretly testing other agents. There is also the line of thought that the TSA is really just doing 'security theater'. This means the real act of stopping bad otucomes comes from intelligence outside the TSA, and the TSA is just a to make people feel safe. On the other hand, Israel uses very tough methods of racially profiling people, making security the first thing you go through when entering the airport, a very personal interview, and totally willing invade privacy and to make people open their computers, phones, etc. If you have ever walked through an airport you have probably seen "duty free" on a few stores. This is because there is a quirk in tax law in airports where the items sold are not subject to normal sales taxes. This is probably a bigger deal in European countries where the VAT is relatively high, and of course this matters more for luxury products, which if you are flying, you are way more likely to buy because you have a higher than average income. The prices have always seemed inflated to me anyway, presumably because of a similar logic of stadium food, but also I don't buy the luxury items they have there in the first place. I do think it interesting that Chuck Feeney became a billionaire by starting (with some other people) a retail chain that exclusively does duty-free shops in airports, and then gave away basically all of his fortune to fund education. Lowcost airlines are a thing you might know about from Europe. There are a few things that make low cost airlines common in Europe. The first is that the EU has very strong antitrust laws, which the low cost airlines took advantage of. Another is that the US has a flat fee on every airline ticket that funds the TSA, and this makes cheap tickets very hard to make work. I think it also matters that more airports in Europe are privately owned, meaning more airports are trying to get more traffic to their airport. The low cost airlines generally play hardball with airports, because they aggressively negotiate cheap landing fees, and threaten to fly to a nearby airport instead. Low cost airlines also charge for just about everything, where as other lines will routinely give free checked bags or complimentary snacks and drinks on the flight. Low cost airlines also will not always use the gates and instead require passengers to walk from the plane to the airport, because going to a gate and using buses for passenger convenience costs the airline money. One weird thing that has happened is that large firms own large slices of each major airline in the United States. Because of this, Azar, Schmalz, and Tecu find that common ownership of airlines is anti-competitive, but there are private gains because the owners of airlines have high profits. This raises some interesting questions about firms like Vanguard and Blackrock that I won't get into right now. But it also raise the interesting question about whether airline tickets should be cheaper or more expensive given carbon emissions of air travel. To start with, air plane emissions are about 2-3% of all emissions, based on the most recent data. It is not the biggest chunk by far, but in the long term, there needs to be movement on this somehow if the goal is net zero emissions. So obviously higher fuel taxes and thus more expensive air travel would reduce emissions. On the other hand we generally don't like anti-competitive behavior in an industry, because it results in some combination of higher prices and worse service relative to the counterfactual more competitive alternative. Jumping back to the specifics of airlines, fuel taxes should be higher, if no other reason than just correcting the unpriced externalities (and a more efficient way to tax). But there also needs to be better alternatives, mainly trains, but also buses like Greyhound or Flixbus. And that requires a lot more state support to shift people to these alternatives and to make these alternatives more viable. Ideally you probably want to make it so no one flies less than 500 km (311 miles) or so. Or if you do, make it very very expensive. And conveniently, this is the range over which buses and trains are at their best relative to both cars and planes. Trains

There is electricity to trains, (and substations) which face normal power tradeoffs. AC power can have greater distances between substations. DC voltage is more efficient but transmission is more expensive (related to why most metros are DC voltage?). DC voltage is better for longer distances too? The Netherlands choose 1.5 kV DC because it was easier to have lower voltage and provided more benefits over AC.

Diesel electric trains are very common, meaning that diesel powers the train, but the power output is put into a DC generator and then DC motors, which then powers the train wheels. This is because DC motors are easy to control and start with high torque.They are also cheap to manufacture and operate.

To get power to trains, you can either use catenary and pantographs (overhead wires) or use a third rail. Overhead wires also have a mechanism to rotate the wires so the wire doesn't wear out on one side from repeated contact with the train. Third rails are common for metro systems. They do have to have more protection because of the live electricity, so they are not as common on longer or intercity lines.

Then there is the electric power on board and to the motors, which will run power from the external source into rectifiers and inverters plus other things to get the desired voltage and current to the electric motors (and also convert some power for on board lights, screens, outlets, etc.). Here are some diagrams of AC and DC electrification, assuming the motor runs on DC power.

Electric trains are driven by electric motors, specifically called tractions motors. Traction motors used to be only series wound DC motors because they were easy to control. Series wound DC motors mean that the windings in the armature and the field windings are in series with each other. The field windings are the stationary coils around the stator, and the armature is the electrical connection on the moving rotor connected to the brushes. This results in high current and high starting torque, making it ideal for accelerating a (heavy) train. In the last few decades they are using AC motors. AC motors are used because there are better electronics now to control and optimize the motors, have lower maintenance costs, and have less weight for the same power output. There are both AC induction motors, and synchronous AC motors (like on the TGV in France). Also, permanent magnet synchronous motors. Also single phase vs three phase synchronous AC motors? Note that the power to the electric motors on board can be DC or AC, or even converted from AC to DC, then to AC at the motors. To convert from DC power to AC motors, power is run through an inverter and a variable frequency drive. To convert from AC power to DC motors, the power is run through rectifiers. Magnetic levitation trains apparently use switch reluctance electric motors.

Regenerative breaking 1 and 2. AC is easier to do regenerative braking, even though DC voltage can do it too. More on AC vs DC traction motors.

So I was looking at train electrication and WTF Acela? Why do you have three different electrified systems, including different frequencies and 12 and 12.5 kilovolts on different segments of the route? This led me down a rabbit hole looking at the history of the Northeast Corridor. So the NEC was really merged into Amtrak and all the constituent lines were built by different companies at different times with different technologies and they sometimes copied nearby examples, like the Pennsylvania railroad which copied the New York New Haven and Hartford Railroad lines with 11 kV AC 25 Hz. This electrification was deemed good enough in the early 1900s because we weren’t as good at electrification technology and struggled to make electrification work over longer distances, even though today it is much easier and cheaper and more efficient. This is why basically from New York City to DC and also SEPTA and also parts of lines in Connecticut use 25 Hz, even though the rest of the NEC is 60 Hz. In fact, there was a Connecticut supreme court decision banning third rail because it was too dangerous. This led to some experimentation by the New York New Haven and Hartford Line, where they settled on 11 kilovolts AC as the optimal electrification system, (AC being chosen because of the longer distances and third rail DC not an option). 25 Hz was chosen because there were several other lines already using 25 Hz, even though they thought that 15 Hz was better if you could start from scratch, due to lower losses. In the 1970s there was a plan to move the 25 Hz system to 60 Hz, but was deemed to expensive. Instead the voltage was increased from 11 kV to 12 kV. Also, part of the NEC in New Jersey was originally the 11 kV AC 25 Hz, but was moved to 12 kV 25 Hz AC in the 1970s, and now is 25 kV 60 Hz AC. Metro North, which shares some of the NEC track, was converted from the 11 kV AC 25 Hz to 12 kV 25 Hz AC, and then upgraded to 12.5 kV AC 60 Hz in the late 1980s when a power station got shut down for some reason? In the early 1990s, Congress passed a law to convert Boston to New York on the NEC to 25 kV AC 60 Hz. This was done to improve Amtrak solvency and reduce trip times. So that is a basic outline of why, which then leads to the question of why not standardize to 25 kV AC instead of 12.5 kV AC or go even further and make the whole NEC 25 kV AC? The most obvious answer is that rewiring the hundreds of miles of 12kV 25 Hz would be very expensive for minimal gain, even though it would make people like me happy to see one unified electrification system. Also, there really isn't anything wrong with the current system other than new trains require slightly higher costs to be able to run on all three electrification systems. Also, increasing the voltage would require more clearance between the overhead wires and the train, which would require a lot of work to make that extra room where ever that situation occurs. I still think it would be worth exploring making the 12.5 kV AC match the 25 kV AC like the rest of the NEC north of New York City, but that requires coordination with Metro-North and would probably have to be funded by Amtrak or the federal government to get them to agree. I would like to see a cost estimate of this though.
